1. Objectives

Microbial electrolysis cells (MECs) convert organic waste and wastewater into
biohydrogen using exoelectrogenic bacteria and only a small electrical input,
making them one of the most direct routes from waste biomass to clean energy
carriers. Their practical yield is limited by the proton exchange membrane (PEM).
In an MEC anolyte, competing cations (Na+, K+, Mg2+, Ca2+) are present at
concentrations roughly 10^5 times higher than protons; they occupy the sulfonate
channels of the membrane, block proton transport, acidify the anode chamber, and
suppress the exoelectrogenic community. A sulfonated poly(arylene ether
sulfone)/polyimide nanofiber (SPAES/PIN) composite membrane has been shown to
outperform Nafion-211 (proton transport number 0.96 vs 0.91; 3-10-fold lower
cation crossover; 32.4% higher MEC hydrogen production), but its authors
explicitly identified two unresolved gaps: (i) there is no validated mechanistic
model that predicts this selectivity, and (ii) the proton-conducting channels of
the SPAES polymer have not been optimized.

This joint project closes both gaps. Its objectives are:
  1. To develop and experimentally validate a molecular-scale mechanistic model
     of proton selectivity, using molecular dynamics (MD) simulation to predict
     proton transport number and cation crossover from channel geometry.
  2. To quantify membrane nanostructure (SAXS, TEM tomography, PALS, AFM) so that
     the MD model is parameterised and validated against measurement rather than
     description.
  3. To optimize the proton-conducting channels by MD screening of degree of
     sulfonation (DS), ion exchange capacity (IEC), and side-chain chemistry,
     confirmed by synthesis and experiment, and to identify the DS/IEC "sweet
     spot".
  4. To demonstrate the resulting membrane in continuous MEC operation on real
     Indonesian organic waste streams, translating membrane design into
     measurable biohydrogen yield.

2. Methodology

The project is built on one common work plan with equivalent effort on both
sides, coupled in a closed predict-verify loop. The Japan-based team (Mabuchi
Laboratory, Tohoku University) performs the molecular dynamics work: atomistic
SPAES/water/ion systems are constructed and equilibrated; proton transport is
computed with the membrane-based empirical valence bond (EVB) method developed by
Prof. Mabuchi in its multistate form (MS-EVB), cross-checked by ab initio MD;
cation permeation free energies are obtained by umbrella sampling; and a
continuum Nernst-Planck/Poisson-Boltzmann description is fitted to the MD output
for engineering-scale prediction. The Indonesia-based team (IPB University and
the Energy and Environmental Research Group, Universitas Indonesia) performs the
experimental work: synthesis of SPAES ionomer families with varied DS, IEC, and
side-chain chemistry; electrospinning and composite fabrication; quantitative
nanostructure characterization; mechanical and transport testing; and MEC
performance testing on real substrates. Structure data flow from Indonesia to
Japan as MD inputs and validation targets; MD predictions flow from Japan to
Indonesia as synthesis targets. The loop closes when simulated and measured
transport agree within the target error, at which point the model becomes a
design tool rather than a description.

3. Expected scientific results of the joint research

A validated, quantitative structure-property-transport model that predicts proton
transport number and cation crossover from channel geometry alone, reproducing
the published benchmarks (t+ = 0.96; 3-10-fold crossover reduction) and then
predicting the behaviour of new variants before they are measured; an
experimentally confirmed DS/IEC/side-chain design map with an identified optimum;
open design guidelines that convert the qualitative "narrower, more branched,
more tortuous channel" narrative into a predictive engineering tool; and a
demonstrated MEC biohydrogen yield improvement on real waste streams. Outputs
include peer-reviewed publications in high-reputation international journals,
joint Japan-Indonesia publications, registered intellectual property, and an
openly documented simulation-experiment protocol deposited in the BRIN National
Scientific Repository.

4. Mode of cooperation including the exchange of people

The two teams are mutually dependent by construction: neither the model nor the
optimization can be completed by one side alone. Cooperation runs through (a)

jointly at international conferences.

5. Expected economic and social impact of the joint research

Indonesia generates very large volumes of organic waste from palm oil, food
processing, and municipal sources. Raising MEC hydrogen yield and membrane
lifetime directly improves the economics of converting that waste into clean
energy, supporting Indonesia's energy-independence agenda under Asta Cita and
Japan's target of an advanced bioeconomy society by 2030. Replacing costly
perfluorinated Nafion with a hydrocarbon composite reduces both membrane cost and
the environmental burden of fluorinated materials. The predictive design tool
shortens the development cycle for future membranes, and the capacity-building
component leaves Indonesia with researchers trained in molecular simulation - a
capability currently concentrated in a small number of laboratories. The work
contributes to SDG 7 (Affordable and Clean Energy), SDG 9 (Industry, Innovation
and Infrastructure), SDG 12 (Responsible Consumption and Production), and SDG 13
(Climate Action).

1. Detailed Description of the Joint Project  (max 6 pages)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1) Problem statement

The decarbonisation agenda has sharply increased demand for low-cost, sustainable
routes to green hydrogen. Microbial electrolysis cells are attractive because
they do not merely produce hydrogen - they produce it from waste. Exoelectrogenic
bacteria growing on the anode oxidise organic substrate into protons, electrons,
and CO2; with a small applied voltage the protons and electrons recombine at the
cathode to give hydrogen. The characteristic half-reactions are:

  Anode:   CH3COOH + 2H2O -> 2CO2 + 8H+ + 8e-;   E0 = -0.28 V (NHE)      (1)
  Cathode: 8H+ + 8e- -> 4H2;                     E0 = -0.42 V (NHE)      (2)

Depending on the microbial consortium, MECs can also yield methane, ethanol,
butanol, hydrogen peroxide, and acetate, which places them squarely within the
bioenergy scope of this call: valorisation of waste materials, closed-loop
resource management, and conversion of biomass into biofuels, biogas, and
bioelectricity.

The bottleneck is the proton exchange membrane. The PEM separates anode and
cathode, prevents short circuits, transports protons to the cathode, and
maintains product purity by blocking gas crossover. But the requirements in an
MEC differ fundamentally from those in a hydrogen fuel cell, because the anode
compartment is a living microbial environment. In the anolyte, cations such as
Na+, K+, Mg2+ and Ca2+ are present at concentrations of order 10^5 times that of
protons. These cations compete for the negatively charged sulfonate channels
(-SO3-) of the membrane. Protons that cannot be transported accumulate in the
anode chamber, the anolyte acidifies, the catholyte alkalises, and the
exoelectrogenic community - the biological engine of the whole device -
deteriorates. Proton selectivity, meaning the ability to pass protons while
excluding competing cations, is therefore the single decisive membrane property
for MEC bioenergy.

Nafion, the perfluorosulfonic acid benchmark, performs poorly against this
requirement: low proton selectivity, high gas and substrate crossover, and high
cost. Sulfonated hydrocarbon polymers - chiefly SPEEK and SPAES - are the leading
alternatives. SPAES shows a markedly different hydrophobic-hydrophilic microphase
separation from Nafion, forming narrower, more branched, and more tortuous
ion-conducting channels, which improves selectivity while lowering crossover.
SPAES alone is mechanically weak, because the high IEC needed for Nafion-comparable
conductivity causes excessive water uptake and swelling, and the membrane
delaminates or tears in service. Embedding electrospun polyimide nanofibers (PIN)
in the centre of the SPAES layer solves this: protons travel through the SPAES
phase while the PIN scaffold carries the mechanical load. The resulting SPAES/PIN
composite outperformed Nafion-211 on every relevant metric - proton transport
number 0.96 vs 0.91, 1.5-fold faster proton transfer, 3-10-fold lower cation
crossover, 2-2.5-fold lower gas permeability - and raised MEC hydrogen production
by 32.4% with hydrogen purity of 90.3% against 61.8% for Nafion-211.

The problem this project addresses is that this success is empirical. Its authors
state two open gaps explicitly, and both block further progress:

  Gap 1 - No validated mechanistic model for proton selectivity. Selectivity was
  explained only qualitatively, from microphase separation imaged by AFM in
  tapping mode, and attributed to "narrower, more branched, more tortuous"
  channels. The authors write that "although no model has yet been identified as
  fully correct, this feature could be explained by the microstructural
  difference." No quantitative transport model links channel geometry to the
  measured t+ = 0.96 or to the 3-10-fold crossover reduction. The explanation is
  descriptive, not predictive - so it cannot be used to design the next membrane.

  Gap 2 - Proton-transport channels not yet optimized. A single SPAES formulation
  was tested (IEC > 1.75 meq/g, DS fixed at 100%). The authors write that "the
  properties of our developed SPAES/PIN composite membrane could be further
  improved through the optimization of proton transport channels in SPAES
  polymer." No parametric study of DS, IEC, or side-chain chemistry exists; one
  point in a large design space has been sampled.

If successful, the contribution to the field is a shift from empirical membrane
discovery to model-guided membrane design for bioenergy conversion - and, through
the demonstration on real Indonesian waste streams, a measurable increase in
biohydrogen yield per unit of organic waste.

3) Scientific and technological challenges and the means and methods to
   overcome them

Challenge 1: Proton transport in these membranes is dominated by the Grotthuss
(structural) mechanism, in which the proton is passed along a hydrogen-bonded
water network rather than carried as a solvated ion. Classical force fields
cannot represent this bond-breaking and bond-forming process, which is precisely
why the reference study could not go beyond a qualitative explanation. The
literature confirms the difficulty: Kreuer (2001) sets out the Grotthuss/vehicular
distinction; Devanathan et al. (2007), Karo et al. (2010), and Knox and Voth
(2010) simulate Nafion morphology but do not deliver a predictive selectivity
model for hydrocarbon membranes under high competing-cation load.

  Means: the Japan-based team applies the membrane-based empirical valence bond
  (EVB) molecular dynamics method developed by Prof. Mabuchi, in its multistate
  form (MS-EVB, after Warshel and Weiss 1980; Wu et al. 2008), which represents
  proton hopping explicitly. Results are cross-checked against ab initio MD
  (DFT-MD) on representative sub-cells, so the empirical parameterisation is
  independently anchored. This capability is the specific reason the collaboration
  is credible: it exists in very few laboratories worldwide.

Challenge 2: Cation exclusion is a free-energy problem, not a geometric one. A
channel that is narrow on average may still admit Mg2+ at defects or at
low-hydration nodes. Simple pore-size arguments therefore cannot predict a
3-10-fold crossover reduction.

  Means: permeation free energies for Na+, K+, Mg2+, and Ca2+ are computed by
  umbrella sampling with the weighted histogram analysis method (Kumar et al.
  1992), giving a potential of mean force along the channel axis for each ion.
  Selectivity then emerges from computed barriers rather than from assumed
  geometry. A continuum Nernst-Planck/Poisson-Boltzmann model is fitted to the MD
  output, informed by ion-activity treatments for ion-exchange polymers (Kamcev
  et al. 2015; Geise et al. 2014), so that engineering-scale predictions can be
  made without running a full MD campaign for every candidate.

Challenge 3: The MD model cannot be validated against AFM images. Validation
requires quantitative geometry - channel width, three-dimensional tortuosity,
connectivity, and free volume - which the source study did not measure.

  Means: the Indonesia-based team adds small-angle X-ray scattering (ionic cluster
  spacing and channel width), TEM tomography (3D connectivity and tortuosity), and
  positron annihilation lifetime spectroscopy (free-volume/void size) to its
  existing FE-SEM, EDX, and AFM capability. These measurements both parameterise
  and falsify the MD model. Where domestic instrument access is unavailable,
  measurement time is contracted to accredited Indonesian facilities, with
  cross-checks performed during research visits to Japan.

Challenge 4: The design space of DS, IEC, and side-chain chemistry is far too
large to explore by synthesis alone, and its axes are coupled - raising DS raises
conductivity but also water uptake and swelling, which degrades mechanical
integrity and eventually selectivity.

  Means: MD screens candidates rapidly and cheaply, predicting water uptake,
  hydrophilic-domain morphology, conductivity, and selectivity for each; only the
  most promising formulations are synthesised. Side-chain routes are drawn from
  established chemistry - hydroxyl-functionalised SPAES (Kwon et al. 2015) and
  n-BuOH-assisted self-organization (Wang et al. 2015) - so the synthetic risk is
  bounded. The PIN reinforcement decouples mechanical strength from IEC, widening
  the accessible design window.

Challenge 5: Laboratory transport data do not guarantee performance in a living
MEC, where biofilm formation, biofouling, and variable real-waste composition
intervene.

  Means: confirmatory two-chamber MEC testing is performed by the Indonesia-based
  team on both synthetic acetate medium and real Indonesian waste streams (palm
  oil mill effluent, food-processing wastewater), with extended-duration runs to
  capture fouling. Discrepancies between predicted and observed behaviour are fed
  back as model refinements rather than discarded.

Methods summary - Japan side (MD): classical MD in GROMACS with OPLS-AA/GAFF for
the polymer and SPC/E water; RESP partial charges from HF/6-31G*; MS-EVB for
proton transport; DFT-MD in CP2K for cross-checking; ReaxFF in LAMMPS for
interfacial degradation; NPT production at 298-353 K and 1 bar with Nose-Hoover
and Parrinello-Rahman coupling; systems of ~50,000-200,000 atoms over 100-500 ns;
umbrella sampling with WHAM for cation PMF; analysis in MDAnalysis and VMD; GPU
HPC resources at the Mabuchi Laboratory.

Methods summary - Indonesia side (experiment): SPAES ionomer families of varied
DS and IEC, with hydroxyl and n-BuOH side-chain routes; PIN produced by
electrospinning at ~200 nm average fiber diameter; composite casting from
N,N-dimethylacetamide solution onto the PIN substrate, dried at 80 degC for 6 h,
acidified in 1 N boiling sulfuric acid for 2 h and washed; structure by FE-SEM
with EDX, AFM in tapping mode, SAXS, TEM tomography, and PALS; mechanical testing
per ASTM D882 at 50 mm/min on at least three 50 x 20 mm specimens; proton
transport number in a two-chamber cell against an Ag/AgCl reference electrode
(+0.195 V vs NHE) with a data-acquisition multimeter; cation crossover in a
three-chamber cell by ion chromatography (Na+, K+, Ca2+, Mg2+ at 100 mg/L); gas
crossover by GC with thermal conductivity detection on a Porapak Q column under
99.999% nitrogen; proton conductivity by two-probe electrochemical impedance
spectroscopy; MEC testing in two-chamber reactors (180 mL per chamber) with
carbon felt anode (25 cm2) and Pt-loaded perforated titanium cathode
(0.5 mg Pt/cm2) at 0.5 V applied. Nafion-211 is the control throughout.

4) Future Prospect of Research

Economic contribution. Indonesia produces very large volumes of organic residue -
palm oil mill effluent, agro-industrial and food-processing wastewater, and
municipal organic waste. A membrane that raises MEC hydrogen yield and extends
service life shifts the economics of waste-to-hydrogen conversion, and replacing
perfluorinated Nafion with a hydrocarbon composite reduces membrane cost, which
is one of the dominant capital items in MEC stacks. A predictive design tool
compresses the development cycle for subsequent membrane generations, and the
same tool applies beyond MECs to microbial fuel cells, electrodialysis, and
low-temperature electrolysers.

Social and environmental contribution. Waste-to-hydrogen conversion addresses two
problems at once: organic pollution loading and fossil-derived hydrogen. Reduced
reliance on fluorinated polymers lowers persistent-chemical burden. Distributed
MEC units are well suited to Indonesian agro-industrial sites, supporting local
energy access and the national energy-independence agenda under Asta Cita.

Synergy and mutual benefit. The synergy here is structural, not rhetorical. Japan
holds a world-leading capability in EVB molecular dynamics of proton transport in
membranes but requires a controlled, well-characterised family of hydrocarbon
membranes and real bioenergy operating data to validate and extend it. Indonesia
holds the synthesis and characterization capability and, critically, the real
waste feedstocks and the deployment need, but requires the predictive model to
escape empirical trial-and-error. Neither side can complete the work alone, and
each gains a capability it does not currently have: Indonesia gains molecular
simulation expertise seeded by three doctorally-trained ECRs; Japan gains
validation of its EVB method on a new polymer class under realistic bioenergy
conditions, and access to field data unavailable domestically.

Long-term trajectory. Beyond the funded 36 months, the model is intended to be
extended to multi-cation anolytes and full operating envelopes, then inverted -
specifying a target selectivity and letting the model prescribe channel geometry
and DS/IEC - and finally used to build a membrane library for scale-up and
deployment in real MEC installations and other PEM applications. Results are
expected to disseminate through the ASEAN region, in line with the aims of the
NEXUS framework.

2. Time Schedule and Research Plan  (max 4 pages)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Project duration: 36 months, April 2027 - March 2030 (fiscal years 2027, 2028,
2029). The two sides operate one common work plan; each year has a defined
handover point in both directions, so that neither team can proceed without the
other's output.

Overview of the common work plan
  Year 1: build both platforms; establish the baseline in simulation and in
          measurement; produce the first controlled DS/IEC family.
  Year 2: complete and first-validate the mechanistic model (Gap 1); begin
          MD screening of the design space (Gap 2).
  Year 3: complete the design map, synthesise and blind-test the optimum, and
          demonstrate it in continuous MEC operation on real waste.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1st Year (FY2027)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Research Work Plan

1) Japan side
   1.1 Establish and benchmark the GPU-HPC MD workflow for this system: classical
       MD, reactive MD, and DFT-MD engines; force-field assembly for SPAES with
       RESP partial charges.
   1.2 Construct the baseline atomistic SPAES model (DS 100%, IEC > 1.75 meq/g)
       with hydration set to the measured water uptake (35 +/- 3%) and
       neutralising counter-ions (H+, Na+, K+, Mg2+, Ca2+).
   1.3 Energy-minimise and equilibrate (NVT then NPT) until density and
       hydrophilic-domain size are stable; run first production MD.
   1.4 Compute radial distribution functions, water and ion connectivity, and
       channel width and tortuosity for the baseline; compare against the
       Indonesian SAXS/TEM/PALS data as it arrives.
   1.5 Implement and verify the EVB/MS-EVB proton-transport module for the SPAES
       chemistry; validate against DFT-MD on sub-cells.
   HANDOVER TO INDONESIA (end Year 1): first-pass predicted channel geometry and
   preliminary conductivity for the baseline, plus the list of DS/IEC points to
   be synthesised in Year 2.

2) Indonesia side
   1.6 Upgrade characterization capability: establish access and protocols for
       SAXS, TEM tomography, and PALS alongside existing FE-SEM, EDX, and AFM;
       run standards and reference materials to fix measurement uncertainty.
   1.7 Re-fabricate the baseline SPAES/PIN composite and Nafion-211 control;
       reproduce the published baseline properties in-house.
   1.8 Quantitative baseline nanostructure measurement: channel width and cluster
       spacing (SAXS), 3D connectivity and tortuosity (TEM tomography),
       free volume (PALS).
   1.9 Baseline transport and mechanical measurement: t+, cation crossover, gas
       crossover, proton conductivity, tensile strength.
   1.10 Synthesise the first controlled SPAES family with varied DS and IEC;
       electrospin PIN at ~200 nm and fabricate composites.
   1.11 Characterise the Indonesian waste feedstocks (palm oil mill effluent,
       food-processing wastewater) for cation load and organic content, defining
       the realistic operating envelope for the model.
   HANDOVER TO JAPAN (end Year 1): quantitative baseline nanostructure and
   transport dataset with stated uncertainties; measured anolyte cation
   composition to set MD boundary conditions.

Research Exchange Plan

1) Japan side
   - Host the three Indonesian ECR candidates from the start of the doctoral
     programme; deliver structured induction in MD methodology, EVB theory, and
     HPC operation.
   - Host Joint Workshop 1 in Sendai (month 10-12): technical review of baseline
     model and measurement, training session on MD workflow for the Indonesian
     team, open scientific session for the Japanese bioenergy community.
   - Dispatch one postdoctoral researcher and one graduate student to Indonesia
     (2-3 weeks) for training in membrane fabrication and MEC operation and for
     joint waste-feedstock sampling.
   Purpose and expected outcome: transfer simulation capability to the Indonesian
   team, acquire hands-on understanding of the experimental constraints that the
   model must respect, and establish the joint data protocol.

2) Indonesia side
   - Dispatch the three ECR candidates to Tohoku University for doctoral study;
     complete admission, ethics training, and work-package assignment.
   - Dispatch Erdiyanto Munandar (doctoral candidate, UI) and one senior
     researcher to Tohoku (2-3 weeks) for joint analysis of the baseline
     structure-model comparison.
   - Host the Japanese visiting researchers; deliver training in electrospinning,
     composite casting, and MEC assembly and operation.
   - Present the baseline dataset at one international conference, with an ECR as
     presenting author.
   Purpose and expected outcome: begin building domestic molecular-simulation
   capability, ensure the measured dataset is directly usable as MD input, and
   establish international visibility for young researchers.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2nd Year (FY2028)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Research Work Plan

1) Japan side
   2.1 Complete the mechanistic model: compute proton transport by EVB/MS-EVB and
       cation permeation free energies (Na+, K+, Mg2+, Ca2+) by umbrella sampling
       with WHAM for the baseline and for the Year 1 DS/IEC family.
   2.2 Derive t+ and cation crossover from channel geometry; test against the
       published benchmarks (t+ = 0.96; 3-10-fold crossover reduction) and the
       Indonesian measurements.
   2.3 Fit the continuum Nernst-Planck/Poisson-Boltzmann description to the MD
       output for fast engineering-scale prediction.
   2.4 Begin MD screening of the wider design space: DS and IEC grid plus
       side-chain routes (hydroxyl functionalisation; n-BuOH self-organization),
       predicting water uptake, domain morphology, conductivity, and selectivity
       for each candidate.
   2.5 Run reactive MD on interfacial degradation to bound expected membrane
       lifetime.
   HANDOVER TO INDONESIA (mid Year 2 and end Year 2): validated model plus a
   ranked shortlist of candidate formulations for synthesis, each with a
   quantitative predicted performance and stated confidence.

2) Indonesia side
   2.6 Complete characterization and transport testing of the full Year 1 DS/IEC
       family; deliver the structure-transport dataset that validates or falsifies
       the model.
   2.7 Establish the side-chain synthesis routes (hydroxyl-functionalised SPAES;
       n-BuOH-assisted self-organization) and verify by NMR, FTIR, and IEC
       titration.
   2.8 Synthesise the first tranche of MD-shortlisted formulations; fabricate and
       characterise composites.
   2.9 Commission the MEC test rigs for extended-duration operation; establish
       reproducible baseline hydrogen yield, purity, and coulombic efficiency on
       synthetic acetate medium and on one real waste stream.
   2.10 Feed all discrepancies between prediction and measurement back to the
       Japan side as structured model-refinement requests.
   HANDOVER TO JAPAN (throughout Year 2): validation dataset for the DS/IEC
   family; synthesis feasibility constraints that bound the design space.

Research Exchange Plan

1) Japan side
   - Send two researchers (including one ECR) to Joint Workshop 2 in Bogor/Depok
     (month 22-24), presenting the validated model and the design map in
     progress.
   - Dispatch one graduate student to Indonesia (4-6 weeks) to work alongside the
     experimental team during the validation campaign, so that model refinement
     is informed by direct observation of the measurement.
   - Co-supervise the Indonesian master's students remotely on simulation-adjacent
     analysis tasks.
   Purpose and expected outcome: ensure the model is refined against measurement
   rather than against assumptions; deepen the working relationship at
   early-career level; produce the first joint publication.

2) Indonesia side
   - Host Joint Workshop 2 in Bogor/Depok, including an open session for
     Indonesian bioenergy researchers and industry, and a training module on
     molecular simulation delivered jointly with the Tohoku team and the ECRs.
   - Dispatch two researchers to Tohoku (3-4 weeks) for joint interpretation of
     the design-space screening results.
   - ECR candidates present at one international conference each; at least one
     first-author ECR presentation.
   - Submit the first joint Japan-Indonesia publication (mechanistic model).
   Purpose and expected outcome: disseminate the method within Indonesia, secure
   the first joint output, and consolidate ECR international visibility.

3rd Year (FY2029)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Research Work Plan

1) Japan side
   3.1 Complete the DS/IEC/side-chain design map; identify the predicted optimum
       and the accessible design window that preserves mechanical integrity.
   3.2 Issue blind predictions for the optimum and for at least two control
       variants, recorded before the Indonesian measurements are made.
   3.3 Quantify predictive accuracy against the blind measurements; perform final
       force-field refinement where systematic deviation is found.
   3.4 Extend the model to the measured real-anolyte cation composition and to the
       full operating temperature range.
   3.5 Publish the model, the design map, and the simulation protocol as an openly
       documented, reusable framework.

2) Indonesia side
   3.6 Synthesise and fabricate the MD-identified optimum formulation and the

   3.7 Perform the blind validation campaign: full structure, mechanical, and

   3.8 Demonstrate the optimum membrane in continuous MEC operation on real
       Indonesian waste streams, including extended-duration runs to capture
       biofouling; measure hydrogen yield, purity, coulombic efficiency, and
       membrane durability against the Nafion-211 control and the published
       SPAES/PIN baseline.
   3.9 Deposit primary data and the data management plan in the BRIN National
       Scientific Repository; deposit materials and specimens with the BRIN
       Directorate of Scientific Collection Management.
   3.10 Prepare and file joint intellectual property on the optimized composition
       and/or fabrication route under the collaborative agreement.

   Purpose and expected outcome: convert the collaboration into a permanent
   institutional link, return trained capability to Indonesia, and deliver the
   full set of mandatory outputs.
