1   RINGKASAN PROPOSAL

        Proton exchange membranes (PEMs) are the heart of microbial electrolysis
cells (MECs), where they must selectively transport protons while excluding the
competing cations (Na+, K+, Mg2+, Ca2+) that coexist at concentrations typically
10^5 times higher than protons. A recent SPAES/polyimide nanofiber (SPAES/PIN)
composite membrane demonstrated outstanding proton selectivity—proton transport
number of 0.96 and 3-10-fold lower cation crossover than Nafion-211—yet its
authors openly acknowledged two unresolved gaps: (i) there is no validated
mechanistic model that predicts this selectivity, and (ii) the proton-conducting
channels of the SPAES polymer have not yet been optimized. This project directly
addresses those two author-stated gaps. It will build a quantitative,
experimentally validated ion-transport model that links the membrane's
nanostructure (channel width, branching, tortuosity, cluster connectivity) to its
measured transport properties, and it will systematically optimize the
proton-conducting channels by mapping the degree of sulfonation (DS), ion
exchange capacity (IEC), and side-chain chemistry against selectivity and
mechanical strength.
        The project will be led by Korea Maritime and Ocean University, with strong
collaboration from the Green Material Research Group of Kolon Central Research Park.
KMOU will lead the quantitative nanostructure characterization, transport testing,
and model development, while Kolon will lead the parametric synthesis of SPAES
ionomers and side-chain-modified variants. Expected outcomes include scientific
publications, a validated structure-property-transport model, and design guidelines
that convert the previously qualitative "narrower, more branched, more tortuous
channels" narrative into a predictive engineering tool. The project also provides
training for students and young researchers. In the long term, this research
supports the rational, model-guided design of proton-selective membranes and aligns
with the UN Sustainable Development Goals on energy efficiency, innovation, and
climate action.

2   PENDAHULUAN

2.1 Latar belakang masalah

        In recent years, the rapid push toward decarbonisation has been accompanied
by a sharp rise in the demand for reliable, low-cost, and sustainable green-hydrogen
technologies. Microbial electrolysis cells (MECs) are an attractive green energy
technology that convert organic waste into a wide range of value-added products via
specific microbes (i.e., exoelectrogens or biocatalysts) with only a small input of
electric energy. Hydrogen gas is the most common product, but MECs using biocatalysts
can also produce methane, ethanol, butanol, hydrogen peroxide, and acetate. The basic
components of a MEC are two electrodes (anode and cathode), a membrane, exoelectrogens,
and a power supply. In a MEC, exoelectrogens growing on the anode convert the substrate
into protons (H+), electrons (e-), and CO2; the protons and electrons are then
transferred to the cathode and recombine to produce hydrogen under the assistance of the
power supply. Typical half reactions are:
        Anode:   CH3COOH + 2H2O -> 2CO2 + 8H+ + 8e-;  E deg = -0.28 V (NHE)   (1)
        Cathode: 8H+ + 8e- -> 4H2;                    E deg = -0.42 V (NHE)   (2)

        Despite their strategic importance, MECs face distinctive challenges arising
from the proton exchange membrane (PEM). A PEM acts as a separator between the anode and
cathode to avoid short circuits while transporting protons to the cathode, and it is
essential for improving product purity by preventing the interchange of gases (CO2, CO,
CH4, etc.) generated in both chambers. However, the PEM used in MECs requires completely
different characteristics from that used in hydrogen fuel cells, because microorganisms
are used as biocatalysts in the anode. In the anodic solution, various cations (Na+,
Mg2+, Ca2+, etc.) exist at a concentration typically 10^5 times higher than the protons;
these cations hinder proton transport by competing for the negatively charged sulfonate
channels (-HSO3-) of the membrane. As protons lose the chance of being transported, they
accumulate in the anode chamber and cause a serious pH decrease, while a corresponding
pH increase occurs in the cathode chamber. This pH drop harms the anode bacteria and
deteriorates MEC performance. Therefore, proton selectivity—selectively passing protons
while excluding competing cations—is a key requirement for the PEM in MEC applications.

        As a PEM, Nafion (DuPont) is the most widely used; it is a perfluorosulfonic acid
membrane with a hydrophobic fluorocarbon backbone (-CF2-CF2-) and hydrophilic side chains
bearing sulfonic groups (-HSO3). Despite its widespread use, Nafion has several
limitations for MECs: low proton selectivity, a high crossover tendency for gas and
substrate, and high production cost. To overcome these drawbacks, sulfonated hydrocarbon
polymers—mainly sulfonated poly(ether ether ketone) (SPEEK) and sulfonated poly(arylene
ether sulfone) (SPAES)—have been investigated as alternatives. Compared with Nafion,
SPAES shows a markedly different hydrophobic-hydrophilic microphase separation, forming
more branched, narrower, and more tortuous ion-conducting channels via the Grotthuss
mechanism, which improves proton selectivity while lowering undesirable gas or substrate
crossover.

        Although extensive research has been carried out, several knowledge gaps remain.
Despite its advantages, SPAES has an inherent mechanical weakness due to excessive water
uptake (WU) and swelling, because a high ion exchange capacity (IEC) is needed to reach
proton conductivity comparable to Nafion; this excessive sulfonation causes mechanical
instability, so the membrane can easily be delaminated or torn during application. To
solve this weakness while keeping SPAES' superior proton transport, the reference study
developed a SPAES/polyimide nanofiber (SPAES/PIN) composite membrane, in which PINs—
highly compatible with SPAES—are embedded as a substratum in the centre of the SPAES
layer to improve mechanical strength and dimensional stability. Protons are transferred
through the SPAES polymer, while physical stability is maintained by the PINs.

        The SPAES/PIN membrane demonstrated superior selectivity to Nafion-211: a proton
transport number of 0.96 (vs. 0.91), 1.5-fold faster proton transfer, 3-10-fold lower
cation crossover, and 2-2.5-fold lower gas permeability, improving MEC hydrogen
production by 32.4%. Despite this success, the authors explicitly identified two gaps
that remain open, and these two gaps define the scope of the present proposal.

        Research Gap 1 - No validated mechanistic model for proton selectivity
[AUTHOR-STATED]. In the reference study, selectivity was explained only qualitatively:
microphase separation observed by atomic force microscopy (AFM) in tapping mode was used
to attribute the effect to "narrower, more branched, more tortuous" hydrophilic channels
in SPAES relative to Nafion. The authors themselves state that "although no model has yet
been identified as fully correct, this feature could be explained by the microstructural
difference." In other words, there is no quantitative transport model that links the
channel geometry to the measured proton transport number (0.96) or to the 3-10-fold
reduction in cation crossover; the explanation is descriptive, not predictive.

        Without such a model, membrane design remains trial-and-error, and the observed
selectivity cannot be extrapolated to new formulations or operating conditions. This
proposal will close the gap by developing a quantitative ion-transport model (e.g., a
Nernst-Planck/Poisson-Boltzmann framework, or a Grotthuss-vehicular partitioning
description) that predicts the proton transport number (t+) and cation crossover directly
from channel dimensions. It will add quantitative nanostructure characterization beyond
AFM—small-angle X-ray scattering (SAXS), transmission electron microscopy (TEM)
tomography, and positron annihilation—to measure channel width, tortuosity, and cluster
connectivity, and it will correlate the measured microstructure with the transport data
to build a genuine structure-property relationship instead of a qualitative narrative.

        Research Gap 2 - Proton-transport channels not yet optimized [AUTHOR-STATED].
In the reference study, only a single SPAES formulation was used: an ion exchange
capacity (IEC) of >1.75 meq/mg and a degree of sulfonation (DS) fixed at 100%,
polymerized by Kolon. The authors note that "the properties of our developed SPAES/PIN
composite membrane could be further improved through the optimization of proton transport
channels in SPAES polymer." No parametric study of DS, IEC, or side-chain chemistry was
performed, so only one point in a large design space was ever tested. This proposal will
close the gap by systematically varying DS and IEC and mapping the trade-off between
proton conductivity, selectivity, water uptake, and mechanical strength. It will explore
side-chain modification—for example, introducing hydroxyl side groups (as in Kwon et al.
[34]) or self-organization additives such as n-BuOH (as in n-BuOH-treated SPEEK [35])—to
further narrow and branch the hydrophilic channels, and it will identify the DS/IEC
"sweet spot" that maximizes selectivity without sacrificing the mechanical stability
provided by the polyimide nanofiber reinforcement.

        These two gaps are tightly coupled: the optimization in Gap 2 generates the
family of controlled microstructures needed to build and validate the model in Gap 1,
while the model in Gap 1 turns the optimization in Gap 2 from empirical screening into
rational, predictive design. The work is strengthened through collaboration with the
Green Material Research Group of Kolon Central Research Park, which provides advanced
polymer synthesis capability for the DS/IEC and side-chain variants.

        In terms of United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), this research is
aligned with SDG 7 (Affordable and Clean Energy), SDG 9 (Industry, Innovation, and
Infrastructure), and SDG 13 (Climate Action), as it aims to improve energy efficiency,
strengthen clean-hydrogen infrastructure, and reduce the environmental impact of waste
treatment. More importantly, the topic lies at the intersection of two EQUITY research
priority areas—energy and advanced materials—by converting a proven-but-empirical
membrane into a rationally designed, model-guided technology.

2.2 Tujuan

        The long term goal of this research is to enable the rational, model-guided
design of proton-selective hydrocarbon membranes for next-generation microbial
electrolysis cells. In general, the objectives of this study, which map directly onto
the two author-stated research gaps, are focused as follows:
1.  To develop and experimentally validate a quantitative ion-transport model that
    predicts the proton transport number and cation crossover from the membrane's
    channel geometry (addresses Gap 1). The model will be built on a Nernst-Planck/
    Poisson-Boltzmann or Grotthuss-vehicular partitioning framework.
2.  To quantitatively characterise the membrane nanostructure beyond AFM—using SAXS,
    TEM tomography, and positron annihilation—to obtain the channel width, tortuosity,
    and cluster connectivity required as model inputs (supports Gap 1).
3.  To optimize the proton-conducting channels by parametrically varying DS, IEC, and
    side-chain chemistry, and to identify the DS/IEC "sweet spot" that maximizes proton
    selectivity without sacrificing mechanical stability (addresses Gap 2).

3   METODOLOGI

        The research will be mainly conducted at the Department of Environmental
Engineering, College of Engineering, Korea Maritime and Ocean University, together with
the Green Material Research Group of Kolon Central Research Park. The set-up consists of
the membrane fabrication line, the nanostructure characterization suite, the transport
test cells, and the modelling workstation, as illustrated in Figure 1.

        The baseline SPAES/PIN composite PEM is fabricated by impregnating PIN in the
centre of a SPAES polymer solution, following the reference method. The SPAES polymer
solution is prepared using SPAES of >1.75 meq/mg IEC, polymerized by Kolon (South Korea),
and N,N-dimethylacetamide (DMAc) as a solvent; the baseline degree of sulfonation (DS) is
100%. For the optimization study (Gap 2), a family of SPAES ionomers with varied DS and IEC
and with side-chain modification (hydroxyl groups; n-BuOH self-organization) is synthesised
by Kolon. As a supporting substrate, polyimide nanofiber (PIN) is selected for its excellent
mechanical properties and compatibility with SPAES, and it is fabricated by the electrospinning
method with an average fiber thickness of 200 nm. Each composite membrane is prepared by pouring
the SPAES/DMAc solution onto the PIN substrate on a glass plate, followed by natural drying at
80 degC for 6 h on a clean bench, then acidified in a 1 N boiling sulfuric acid solution for 2 h
and washed with deionized water. Nafion-211 (DuPont, USA) is used as the control.

        The membrane microstructure is characterised using a field emission scanning electron
microscope (FE-SEM, TESCAN-MIRA3 LMU, Czech) with energy-dispersive X-ray (EDX) spectroscopy for
composition, and atomic force microscopy (AFM) in soft tapping mode for microphase separation. To
obtain quantitative channel dimensions for the model, small-angle X-ray scattering (SAXS), TEM
tomography, and positron annihilation lifetime spectroscopy (PALS) are added. Mechanical strength
is measured with an Instron 5967 tension tester (Instron Corp., USA), testing at least three
specimens (50 mm x 20 mm) at a crosshead speed of 50 mm/min (ASTM D882).

        Transport properties are measured following the reference protocols. The proton
transport number (t+) is obtained in a two-chambered cell separated by the membrane, using a
Luggin capillary containing an Ag/AgCl reference electrode (+0.195 V vs. NHE; Microelectrode,
USA); the potential difference between chambers (0.01 N and 0.05 N HCl) is recorded with a
multimeter (2700 Data Acquisition Series, Keithley, USA) at 2 s intervals. Cation crossover
(Na+, Ca2+, Mg2+ at 100 mg/L) is measured in a three-chambered cell using ion chromatography
(ICS5000, DIONEX, USA), and gas crossover is measured with a gas chromatograph (Series 580,
GawMac Instrument Co., USA) equipped with a thermal conductivity detector and a Porapak Q sieve
column using nitrogen (99.999%) as the carrier gas. Proton conductivity is determined by
two-probe electrochemical impedance spectroscopy. These measured values become the ground-truth
targets that the transport model must reproduce.

(a) Pelaksanaan penelitian di PT-Pengusul

        The proposed research aims to close the two author-stated gaps of the SPAES/PIN
membrane study. The main activities are listed as follows.

    A.  Upgrading of the Facility for Quantitative Nanostructure Characterization
            The initial step is to upgrade the characterization facility so that channel
        geometry can be measured, not just imaged. Beyond the existing FE-SEM, EDX, and AFM,
        the study integrates small-angle X-ray scattering (SAXS), TEM tomography, and
        positron annihilation lifetime spectroscopy (PALS). SAXS provides the average
        ionic-cluster spacing and channel width; TEM tomography reconstructs the
        three-dimensional connectivity and tortuosity of the hydrophilic network; and PALS
        probes the free-volume/void size. Together these convert the qualitative AFM picture
        (Fig. 6 of the reference study) into quantitative inputs for the transport model,
        directly serving Gap 1.

    B.  Parametric Optimization of the Proton-Transport Channels (Gap 2)
            This work package attacks the second author-stated gap: the channels have only
        been tested at a single DS (100%) and IEC (>1.75 meq/mg). Here, Kolon synthesises a
        controlled family of SPAES ionomers spanning a DS/IEC range, plus side-chain-modified
        variants (hydroxyl side groups after Kwon et al. [34]; n-BuOH self-organization after
        the modified SPEEK of ref [35]). Each variant is fabricated into a SPAES/PIN composite
        and characterised for water uptake, swelling ratio, tensile strength, proton conductivity,
        and proton selectivity.
            The main objective is to map the design space rather than test a single point.
        Parameters (DS, IEC, side-chain type) are varied one by one and then in combination to
        reveal the trade-offs among conductivity, selectivity, water uptake, and mechanical
        strength. The experimental data are analysed to locate critical thresholds—for example,
        the IEC beyond which excessive water uptake causes mechanical instability, and the DS
        that gives the narrowest, most branched channels.
            The outcome is a quantified DS/IEC "sweet spot" that maximizes proton selectivity
        while the embedded polyimide nanofibers preserve mechanical stability. This family of
        controlled microstructures also becomes the calibration and validation set for the model
        in work package C.

    C.  Development and Validation of a Quantitative Ion-Transport Model (Gap 1)
            This work package attacks the first author-stated gap: the absence of a predictive
        model. Using the quantitative channel dimensions from work package A and the controlled
        microstructures from work package B, a transport model is formulated. A Nernst-Planck/
        Poisson-Boltzmann framework describes electromigration and diffusion of protons and
        competing cations through charged, confined channels, while a Grotthuss-vehicular
        partitioning term captures the structural (hopping) vs. vehicular proton transport that
        distinguishes SPAES from Nafion. Channel width, tortuosity, sulfonate charge density,
        and connectivity are the physical inputs.
            The model is validated against the ground-truth transport data (proton transport
        number, cation crossover, proton conductivity) measured in the metodologi. A successful
        model must reproduce the reference benchmarks—t+ = 0.96 and the 3-10-fold cation-crossover
        reduction—from geometry alone, and must then predict the transport of the new DS/IEC and
        side-chain variants before they are measured, as a genuine predictive test.
            The outcome converts the qualitative "narrower, more branched, more tortuous channels"
        explanation into a quantitative structure-property relationship, giving a design tool that
        can be extrapolated to new formulations and operating conditions.

    D.  Structure-Property-Transport Correlation and Design Guidelines
            The final work package integrates A-C into a single structure-property-transport
        correlation. The relationship between measured channel geometry, membrane composition
        (DS/IEC/side chain), and transport performance is consolidated into design guidelines and
        a predictive workflow. Sensitivity analysis identifies which structural features most
        strongly govern selectivity, guiding future membrane design. The outcome is a documented,
        validated framework that closes both author-stated gaps and provides a reusable tool for
        rational membrane design.

            This collaboration will focus on the ground-truth transport testing that underpins the
        model: proton transport number measurement (two-chambered cell with Ag/AgCl reference electrode,
        Keithley 2700 multimeter), cation crossover (three-chambered cell, ICS5000 ion chromatography),
        and proton conductivity (two-probe electrochemical impedance spectroscopy). Dr. Park will lead
        the design of the validation experiments so that each modelled quantity has a matched, accurate
        measurement. The study will also assess how the optimized DS/IEC variants behave under
        MEC-relevant conditions, connecting the model back to real bioelectrochemical operation. Such an
        evaluation ensures that the predictive framework remains grounded in the actual application.
            In the next stage, several optimized membrane variants will be selected for confirmatory
        transport testing. Their measured t+ and crossover values will be compared blind against the
        model predictions to quantify predictive accuracy. This step provides the decisive test of
        whether the qualitative narrative has been successfully replaced by a predictive model.

    2)  Kolon Central Research Park (Green Material Research Group)
            The second collaboration is with Dr. Mooseok Lee of the Green Material Research Group, Kolon
        Central Research Park. His expertise covers advanced polymer synthesis, sulfonated poly(arylene
        ether sulfone) polymerization, and composite membrane engineering, with a strong track record in
        controlling the degree of sulfonation and ion exchange capacity of ionomers. With this background,
        Dr. Lee will lead the parametric synthesis that makes the optimization in Gap 2 possible.
            This collaboration will focus on synthesising a controlled family of SPAES ionomers spanning
        a DS/IEC range (around and beyond the baseline >1.75 meq/mg, DS 100%), together with side-chain-
        modified variants—hydroxyl side groups after Kwon et al. [34] and n-BuOH self-organization after
        the modified SPEEK of ref [35]. Based on his expertise in polymer synthesis, Dr. Lee will tune the
        ionomer chemistry and the electrospun polyimide nanofiber support (~200 nm) to deliberately narrow
        and branch the hydrophilic channels. The study will assess how each chemical modification changes
        the channel geometry (verified by SAXS/TEM at KMOU) and the resulting selectivity and mechanical
        strength.
            In the next stage, the most promising variants that define the DS/IEC "sweet spot" will be
        scaled for confirmatory testing. Their reproducibility and stability will be assessed to ensure the
        optimized formulations are practical. Ultimately, this collaboration provides both the material
        design space and the validation samples that connect Gap 2 (optimization) to Gap 1 (model).

APPENDIX A - THE TWO AUTHOR-STATED RESEARCH GAPS AND BASELINE TARGETS
================================================================================

A.1  Author-stated gaps this proposal addresses (verbatim basis)

    Gap 1 - No validated mechanistic model for proton selectivity.
        In the source paper, selectivity was explained qualitatively via microphase
    separation observed by AFM in tapping mode (Fig. 6), attributing it to "narrower,
    more branched, more tortuous" hydrophilic channels in SPAES vs. Nafion. The authors
    state: "although no model has yet been identified as fully correct, this feature could
    be explained by the microstructural difference." There is no quantitative transport
    model linking channel geometry to the measured proton transport number (0.96) or the
    3-10-fold reduction in cation crossover; the explanation is descriptive, not predictive.

    Gap 2 - Proton-transport channels not yet optimized.
        A single SPAES formulation was used (IEC > 1.75 meq/mg, DS fixed at 100%,
    polymerized by Kolon). The authors state that "the properties of our developed
    SPAES/PIN composite membrane could be further improved through the optimization of
    proton transport channels in SPAES polymer." No parametric study of DS, IEC, or
    side-chain chemistry was performed; only one point in a large design space was tested.

A.2  Baseline measured properties (targets the model must reproduce / optimization must beat)

          membrane and Nafion-211 (mean +/- SD, n = 4; from the source paper)
