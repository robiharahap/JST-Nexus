1   RINGKASAN PROPOSAL

        Proton exchange membranes (PEMs) are the heart of microbial electrolysis
cells (MECs), where they must selectively transport protons while excluding the
competing cations (Na+, K+, Mg2+, Ca2+) that coexist at concentrations typically
10^5 times higher than protons. A recent SPAES/polyimide nanofiber (SPAES/PIN)
composite membrane demonstrated outstanding proton selectivity—proton transport
number of 0.96 and 3-10-fold lower cation crossover than Nafion-211—yet its authors
openly acknowledged two unresolved gaps: (i) there is no validated mechanistic model
that predicts this selectivity, and (ii) the proton-conducting channels of the SPAES
polymer have not yet been optimized. This project closes both gaps by combining
molecular dynamics (MD) simulations with experiments. MD builds a molecular-scale,
mechanistic picture of how protons and competing cations move through the sulfonated
channels, while experiments provide the nanostructure and transport data that
calibrate and validate the simulations. The two methods form a closed loop: MD
predicts, experiments verify, and the validated model is then used to optimize the
degree of sulfonation (DS), ion exchange capacity (IEC), and side-chain chemistry.
        The project will be led by Korea Maritime and Ocean University, with strong
collaboration from the Green Material Research Group of Kolon Central Research Park.
KMOU will lead the MD simulations, nanostructure characterization, and transport
testing, while Kolon will lead the parametric synthesis of the SPAES ionomers and the
side-chain-modified variants that the simulations recommend. Expected outcomes include
scientific publications, a validated MD-based structure-property-transport model, an
optimized DS/IEC design map, and design guidelines that convert the previously
qualitative "narrower, more branched, more tortuous channels" narrative into a
predictive engineering tool. The project also provides training for students and
young researchers in both computational and experimental membrane science. In the long
term, this research supports the rational, model-guided design of proton-selective
membranes and aligns with the UN Sustainable Development Goals on energy efficiency,
innovation, and climate action.

2   PENDAHULUAN

2.1 Latar belakang masalah

        In recent years, the rapid push toward decarbonisation has been accompanied
by a sharp rise in the demand for reliable, low-cost, and sustainable green-hydrogen
technologies. Microbial electrolysis cells (MECs) are an attractive green energy
technology that convert organic waste into a wide range of value-added products via
specific microbes (i.e., exoelectrogens or biocatalysts) with only a small input of
electric energy. Hydrogen gas is the most common product, but MECs using biocatalysts
can also produce methane, ethanol, butanol, hydrogen peroxide, and acetate. The basic
components of a MEC are two electrodes (anode and cathode), a membrane, exoelectrogens,
and a power supply. In a MEC, exoelectrogens growing on the anode convert the substrate
into protons (H+), electrons (e-), and CO2; the protons and electrons are then
transferred to the cathode and recombine to produce hydrogen under the assistance of the
power supply. Typical half reactions are:
        Anode:   CH3COOH + 2H2O -> 2CO2 + 8H+ + 8e-;  E deg = -0.28 V (NHE)   (1)
        Cathode: 8H+ + 8e- -> 4H2;                    E deg = -0.42 V (NHE)   (2)

        Despite their strategic importance, MECs face distinctive challenges arising
from the proton exchange membrane (PEM). A PEM acts as a separator between the anode and
cathode to avoid short circuits while transporting protons to the cathode, and it is
essential for improving product purity by preventing the interchange of gases (CO2, CO,
CH4, etc.) generated in both chambers. However, the PEM used in MECs requires completely
different characteristics from that used in hydrogen fuel cells, because microorganisms
are used as biocatalysts in the anode. In the anodic solution, various cations (Na+,
Mg2+, Ca2+, etc.) exist at a concentration typically 10^5 times higher than the protons;
these cations hinder proton transport by competing for the negatively charged sulfonate
channels (-HSO3-) of the membrane. As protons lose the chance of being transported, they
accumulate in the anode chamber and cause a serious pH decrease, while a corresponding
pH increase occurs in the cathode chamber. This pH drop harms the anode bacteria and
deteriorates MEC performance. Therefore, proton selectivity—selectively passing protons
while excluding competing cations—is a key requirement for the PEM in MEC applications.

        As a PEM, Nafion (DuPont) is the most widely used; it is a perfluorosulfonic acid
membrane with a hydrophobic fluorocarbon backbone (-CF2-CF2-) and hydrophilic side chains
bearing sulfonic groups (-HSO3). Despite its widespread use, Nafion has several
limitations for MECs: low proton selectivity, a high crossover tendency for gas and
substrate, and high production cost. To overcome these drawbacks, sulfonated hydrocarbon
polymers—mainly sulfonated poly(ether ether ketone) (SPEEK) and sulfonated poly(arylene
ether sulfone) (SPAES)—have been investigated as alternatives. Compared with Nafion,
SPAES shows a markedly different hydrophobic-hydrophilic microphase separation, forming
more branched, narrower, and more tortuous ion-conducting channels via the Grotthuss
mechanism, which improves proton selectivity while lowering undesirable gas or substrate
crossover.

        Although extensive research has been carried out, several knowledge gaps remain.
Despite its advantages, SPAES has an inherent mechanical weakness due to excessive water
uptake (WU) and swelling, because a high ion exchange capacity (IEC) is needed to reach
proton conductivity comparable to Nafion; this excessive sulfonation causes mechanical
instability, so the membrane can easily be delaminated or torn during application. To
solve this weakness while keeping SPAES' superior proton transport, the reference study
developed a SPAES/polyimide nanofiber (SPAES/PIN) composite membrane, in which PINs—
highly compatible with SPAES—are embedded as a substratum in the centre of the SPAES
layer to improve mechanical strength and dimensional stability. Protons are transferred
through the SPAES polymer, while physical stability is maintained by the PINs.

        The SPAES/PIN membrane demonstrated superior selectivity to Nafion-211: a proton
transport number of 0.96 (vs. 0.91), 1.5-fold faster proton transfer, 3-10-fold lower
cation crossover, and 2-2.5-fold lower gas permeability, improving MEC hydrogen
production by 32.4%. Despite this success, the authors explicitly identified two gaps
that remain open, and these two gaps—each closed here by combined molecular dynamics
(MD) simulations and experiments—define the scope of the present proposal.

        Research Gap 1 - No validated mechanistic model for proton selectivity
[AUTHOR-STATED].
        What the paper did: selectivity was explained only qualitatively; microphase
separation observed by atomic force microscopy (AFM) in tapping mode (Fig. 6 of the
reference study) was used to attribute the effect to "narrower, more branched, more
tortuous" hydrophilic channels in SPAES relative to Nafion.
        The gap: the authors themselves state that "although no model has yet been
identified as fully correct, this feature could be explained by the microstructural
difference." There is no quantitative transport model linking channel geometry to the
measured proton transport number (0.96) or the 3-10-fold reduction in cation crossover.
The explanation is descriptive, not predictive.

        How it is closed here (MD + experiments):
        - Develop a quantitative, molecular-scale transport model. Molecular dynamics
          (MD) simulations resolve proton hopping (Grotthuss) and vehicular transport,
          water/ion distribution, and cation partitioning inside the sulfonated
          channels, yielding the proton transport number (t+) and cation crossover from
          first principles; a continuum Nernst-Planck/Poisson-Boltzmann description is
          fitted to the MD results for engineering-scale prediction.
        - Add quantitative nanostructure characterization beyond AFM—small-angle X-ray
          scattering (SAXS), transmission electron microscopy (TEM) tomography, and
          positron annihilation lifetime spectroscopy (PALS)—to measure channel width,
          tortuosity, and cluster connectivity; these both parameterise and validate the
          MD models.
        - Correlate the simulated and measured microstructure with the transport data to
          build a genuine structure-property relationship instead of a qualitative
          narrative, requiring the MD model to reproduce the experimental t+ = 0.96 and
          the 3-10-fold cation-crossover drop.

        Research Gap 2 - Proton-transport channels not yet optimized [AUTHOR-STATED].
        What the paper did: a single SPAES formulation was used (IEC > 1.75 meq/mg,
degree of sulfonation DS fixed at 100%, polymerized by Kolon).
        The gap: the authors note that "the properties of our developed SPAES/PIN
composite membrane could be further improved through the optimization of proton
transport channels in SPAES polymer." No parametric study of DS, IEC, or side-chain
chemistry was performed; only one point in a large design space was tested.
        How it is closed here (MD + experiments):
        - Systematically vary DS and IEC in silico and in the laboratory, mapping the
          trade-off between proton conductivity, selectivity, water uptake, and
          mechanical strength. MD screens many candidate structures rapidly; experiments
          confirm the most promising ones.
        - Explore side-chain modification (e.g., hydroxyl groups as in Kwon et al. [34],
          or self-organization additives such as n-BuOH-treated SPEEK [35]) to further
          narrow and branch the hydrophilic channels, first modelled by MD, then
          synthesised by Kolon.
        - Identify the DS/IEC "sweet spot" that maximizes selectivity without sacrificing
          the mechanical stability provided by the polyimide nanofiber reinforcement.

        These two gaps are tightly coupled and are addressed with the same MD-plus-
experiment loop: the optimization in Gap 2 generates the family of controlled
microstructures needed to build and validate the MD model in Gap 1, while the validated
MD model in Gap 1 turns the optimization in Gap 2 from empirical screening into rational,
predictive design. The work is strengthened through collaboration with the Green Material
Research Group of Kolon Central Research Park, which provides the advanced polymer
synthesis capability for the DS/IEC and side-chain variants.

        In terms of United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), this research is
aligned with SDG 7 (Affordable and Clean Energy), SDG 9 (Industry, Innovation, and
Infrastructure), and SDG 13 (Climate Action), as it aims to improve energy efficiency,
strengthen clean-hydrogen infrastructure, and reduce the environmental impact of waste
treatment. More importantly, the topic lies at the intersection of two EQUITY research
priority areas—energy and advanced materials—by converting a proven-but-empirical
membrane into a rationally designed, model-guided technology.

2.2 Tujuan

        The long term goal of this research is to enable the rational, model-guided
design of proton-selective hydrocarbon membranes for next-generation microbial
electrolysis cells. In general, the objectives of this study, which map directly onto
the two author-stated research gaps and are pursued by combined MD simulations and
experiments, are focused as follows:
1.  To develop and experimentally validate a molecular-scale mechanistic model of proton
    selectivity using MD simulations that predict the proton transport number and cation
    crossover from channel geometry (addresses Gap 1).
2.  To quantitatively characterise the membrane nanostructure (SAXS, TEM tomography,
    PALS, AFM) to parameterise and validate the MD model (supports Gap 1).
3.  To optimize the proton-conducting channels by MD screening of DS, IEC, and side-chain
    chemistry, confirmed by synthesis and experiments, and to identify the DS/IEC "sweet
    spot" (addresses Gap 2).

3.1 Molecular dynamics (MD) simulation methodology

        The simulations build an atomistic picture of the SPAES hydrophilic channels and
compute the transport of protons and competing cations. Atomistic models of the SPAES
ionomer (baseline DS 100%, IEC > 1.75 meq/mg) are constructed with hydrated sulfonate
groups, and the water content is set from the measured water uptake (35 +/- 3%). Classical
MD is run in GROMACS 2023 with the OPLS-AA and General Amber Force Field (GAFF) for the
polymer and the SPC/E water model; atomic partial charges are derived by the RESP method
from HF/6-31G* calculations in Gaussian 16. Because proton transport in SPAES is dominated
by the Grotthuss (structural) mechanism, proton hopping is treated with the multistate
empirical valence bond (MS-EVB) method, cross-checked by ab initio MD (AIMD/DFT-MD) in
CP2K 2024 on representative sub-cells. Reactive simulations of interfacial degradation use
ReaxFF in LAMMPS. Production runs use the NPT ensemble (Nose-Hoover thermostat,
Parrinello-Rahman barostat) at 298-353 K and 1 bar. Trajectories are analysed with
MDAnalysis and VMD, and free energies of cation permeation are obtained by umbrella
sampling with the weighted histogram analysis method (WHAM). All runs use a GPU HPC
cluster (NVIDIA A100 nodes).

        Table 1. Molecular dynamics simulation setup
+-----------------------------+--------------------------------------------------------+
| Item                        | Specification                                          |
+-----------------------------+--------------------------------------------------------+
| Classical MD engine         | GROMACS 2023 (GPU)                                      |
| Reactive / AIMD engines     | LAMMPS (ReaxFF); CP2K 2024 (DFT-MD)                     |
| Force field (polymer)       | OPLS-AA / GAFF                                          |
| Water model                 | SPC/E (cross-check TIP3P)                               |
| Proton transport            | Multistate empirical valence bond (MS-EVB)             |
| Partial charges             | RESP from HF/6-31G* (Gaussian 16)                       |
| Ensemble / thermostat       | NPT; Nose-Hoover / Parrinello-Rahman                   |
| Temperature / pressure      | 298-353 K / 1 bar                                      |
| System size / time          | ~50,000-200,000 atoms; 100-500 ns (classical)          |
| Free-energy method          | Umbrella sampling + WHAM (cation PMF)                   |
| Analysis                    | MDAnalysis, VMD (RDF, MSD -> diffusion, connectivity)  |
| Hardware                    | GPU HPC cluster (NVIDIA A100 nodes)                    |
+-----------------------------+--------------------------------------------------------+

        The MD workflow proceeds in the following steps:
        Step 1. Build the atomistic SPAES model at a target DS/IEC; add water to the
                measured uptake and neutralising counter-ions (H+, Na+, Mg2+, Ca2+).
        Step 2. Energy-minimise, then equilibrate (NVT then NPT) until density and
                hydrophilic-domain size are stable.
        Step 3. Run production MD; compute radial distribution functions, water/ion
                connectivity, and channel width/tortuosity.
        Step 4. Compute proton transport (MS-EVB/AIMD) and cation permeation free energy
                (umbrella sampling) to obtain t+ and crossover.
        Step 5. Fit a continuum Nernst-Planck/Poisson-Boltzmann model to the MD outputs
                for engineering-scale prediction, and pass results to experiment for
                validation.

3.2 Experimental methodology

        The baseline SPAES/PIN composite PEM is fabricated by impregnating polyimide
nanofiber (PIN) in the centre of a SPAES polymer solution, following the reference method.
The SPAES polymer solution is prepared using SPAES of > 1.75 meq/mg IEC, polymerized by
Kolon (South Korea), and N,N-dimethylacetamide (DMAc) as a solvent; the baseline degree of
sulfonation (DS) is 100%. For the optimization study (Gap 2), a family of SPAES ionomers
with varied DS and IEC and with side-chain modification (hydroxyl groups; n-BuOH
self-organization) is synthesised by Kolon. PIN is fabricated by electrospinning with an
average fiber thickness of 200 nm. Nafion-211 (DuPont, USA) is the control.

        The experimental protocol proceeds in the following steps:
        Step 1. Cast the SPAES/DMAc solution onto the PIN substrate on a glass plate;
                natural drying at 80 degC for 6 h on a clean bench.
        Step 2. Acidify in a 1 N boiling sulfuric acid solution for 2 h; wash with
                deionized water.
        Step 3. Characterise structure: FE-SEM (TESCAN-MIRA3 LMU, Czech) with EDX; AFM
                (tapping mode); SAXS, TEM tomography, and PALS for quantitative channel
                geometry (MD inputs/validation).
        Step 4. Measure mechanical strength with an Instron 5967 tension tester (Instron
                Corp., USA); >= 3 specimens (50 mm x 20 mm), 50 mm/min (ASTM D882).
        Step 5. Measure transport: proton transport number (t+) in a two-chambered cell
                with an Ag/AgCl reference electrode (+0.195 V vs. NHE; Microelectrode,
                USA) and a multimeter (2700 Data Acquisition Series, Keithley, USA);
                cation crossover (Na+, Ca2+, Mg2+, 100 mg/L) in a three-chambered cell by
                ion chromatography (ICS5000, DIONEX, USA); gas crossover with a gas
                chromatograph (Series 580, GawMac Instrument Co., USA) with a thermal
                conductivity detector and a Porapak Q sieve column, nitrogen carrier gas
                (99.999%); proton conductivity by two-probe electrochemical impedance
                spectroscopy.
        Step 6. Confirmatory MEC test: two-chambered MEC (180 mL each), carbon felt anode
                (25 cm^2), Pt-loaded perforated titanium plate cathode (0.5 mg Pt/cm^2),
                acetate substrate (3 mM), 0.5 V applied; gas sampling with a gastight
                syringe (Hamilton SampleLock #1002, 2.5 mL, USA).
        Step 7. Feed the measured structure and transport back to the MD model (Section
                3.1) to validate predictions and refine the optimization.

(a) Pelaksanaan penelitian di PT-Pengusul

        The proposed research aims to close the two author-stated gaps of the SPAES/PIN
membrane study using MD simulations and experiments. The main activities are as follows.

    A.  Building the MD Platform and Upgrading the Characterization Facility
            The initial step establishes both computational and experimental
        infrastructure. On the MD side, the GPU HPC workflow (GROMACS 2023, LAMMPS/ReaxFF,
        CP2K 2024, Gaussian 16 for RESP charges, MS-EVB for proton transport) is set up and
        benchmarked on the baseline SPAES/PIN. On the experimental side, beyond the existing
        FE-SEM, EDX, and AFM, the study integrates SAXS, TEM tomography, and PALS so that
        channel geometry can be measured, not just imaged. SAXS gives the ionic-cluster
        spacing and channel width; TEM tomography reconstructs the 3D connectivity and
        tortuosity; PALS probes the free-volume/void size. Together these convert the
        qualitative AFM picture (Fig. 6 of the reference study) into quantitative inputs and
        validation targets for the MD model, directly serving Gap 1.

    B.  MD-Based Mechanistic Modelling of Proton Selectivity (Gap 1)
            This work package attacks the first author-stated gap: the absence of a
        predictive model. Atomistic SPAES/water/ion systems are built and equilibrated, and
        production MD quantifies the hydrophilic-channel morphology (width, branching,
        tortuosity, connectivity). Proton transport is computed with MS-EVB (cross-checked by
        AIMD in CP2K), and the permeation free energy of each competing cation (Na+, Mg2+,
        Ca2+) is obtained by umbrella sampling. From these, the proton transport number (t+)
        and cation crossover are predicted directly from channel geometry. A continuum
        Nernst-Planck/Poisson-Boltzmann model is then fitted to the MD outputs to allow fast
        engineering-scale prediction.
            The MD predictions are validated against the experimental ground truth: the model
        must reproduce the reference benchmarks—t+ = 0.96 and the 3-10-fold cation-crossover
        reduction—from geometry alone, and must then predict the transport of new variants
        before they are measured. This converts the qualitative "narrower, more branched,
        more tortuous channels" explanation into a quantitative structure-property
        relationship.

    C.  MD-Guided Parametric Optimization of the Channels (Gap 2)
            This work package attacks the second author-stated gap: the channels have only
        been tested at a single DS (100%) and IEC (>1.75 meq/mg). MD is used to screen a
        design space of DS, IEC, and side-chain chemistry (hydroxyl side groups after Kwon
        et al. [34]; n-BuOH self-organization after the modified SPEEK of ref [35]). For each
        candidate, MD predicts water uptake, hydrophilic-domain morphology, proton
        conductivity, and selectivity, revealing the trade-offs against mechanical strength.
        The most promising candidates that define the DS/IEC "sweet spot" are then handed to
        Kolon for synthesis and to the experimental team for confirmation.

    D.  Experimental Synthesis, Fabrication, and Closed-Loop Validation
            The final work package on the pengusul side realises and tests the MD-recommended

        crossover, proton conductivity), and confirmatory MEC hydrogen production. Measured
        values are compared blind against the MD predictions to quantify predictive accuracy
        and to refine the force-field parameters where needed. The outcome is a validated,
        closed MD-plus-experiment framework and documented design guidelines that close both
        author-stated gaps.

8   PETA JALAN (ROAD MAP) RISET

        [Figure 5. Ten years roadmap of MD-guided proton-selective membranes]
        --------------------------------------------------------------------------
        | Phase 1 (2025-2027): Validated MD model + DS/IEC optimization (closing  |
        |     the two author-stated gaps) with experimental validation.           |
        | Phase 2 (2028-2031): MD extended to multi-cation & operating conditions;|
        |     inverse design of channels for a target selectivity.                |
        | Phase 3 (2032-2036): MD-guided membrane library, scale-up, and          |
        |     deployment in real MECs and other PEM applications.                 |
        --------------------------------------------------------------------------

        This figure illustrates a ten-year roadmap. The first phase (2025-2027) closes the two
author-stated gaps by delivering an MD-validated transport model and an optimized DS/IEC design
space, each confirmed by experiment. The second phase (2028-2031) extends the model to
multi-cation anolytes and real operating conditions and moves toward inverse design—specifying a
target selectivity and letting the MD model prescribe the channel geometry and DS/IEC. The third
phase (2032-2036) uses the model to build a membrane library, scale up the best formulations, and
deploy them in real MECs and other PEM applications. This roadmap positions MD-guided design as a
scalable, sustainable path aligned with long-term technological goals.

APPENDIX A - THE TWO AUTHOR-STATED RESEARCH GAPS AND BASELINE TARGETS
================================================================================

A.1  Author-stated gaps this proposal addresses (closed by MD + experiments)

    Gap 1 - No validated mechanistic model for proton selectivity.
        In the source paper, selectivity was explained qualitatively via microphase
    separation observed by AFM in tapping mode (Fig. 6), attributing it to "narrower, more
    branched, more tortuous" hydrophilic channels in SPAES vs. Nafion. The authors state:
    "although no model has yet been identified as fully correct, this feature could be
    explained by the microstructural difference." There is no quantitative transport model
    linking channel geometry to the measured proton transport number (0.96) or the 3-10-fold
    reduction in cation crossover; the explanation is descriptive, not predictive. This
    proposal closes the gap with MD simulations (MS-EVB proton transport, cation PMF)
    validated against SAXS/TEM/PALS structure and measured transport.

    Gap 2 - Proton-transport channels not yet optimized.
        A single SPAES formulation was used (IEC > 1.75 meq/mg, DS fixed at 100%, polymerized
    by Kolon). The authors state that "the properties of our developed SPAES/PIN composite
    membrane could be further improved through the optimization of proton transport channels
    in SPAES polymer." No parametric study of DS, IEC, or side-chain chemistry was performed.
    This proposal closes the gap with MD screening of DS/IEC and side-chain variants, confirmed
    by synthesis (Kolon) and experiment.

Key transport benchmarks (the MD model of Gap 1 must reproduce these from geometry):
        - Proton transport number t+: 0.96 (SPAES/PIN) vs. 0.91 (Nafion-211).
        - Proton transfer rate: 1.5-fold faster for SPAES/PIN.
        - Cation crossover (Na+, Mg2+, Ca2+): 3-10-fold lower for SPAES/PIN.

        - MEC hydrogen production: 32.4% higher; hydrogen purity 90.3% (vs. 61.8%),
          97.2% with methanogen inhibitor (2-bromoethanesulfonate).
