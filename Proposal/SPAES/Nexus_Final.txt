PART II.  ABSTRACT

1.  Objectives
        The long-term goal is to enable the rational, model-guided design of
    proton-selective hydrocarbon membranes for next-generation biohydrogen-producing
    microbial electrolysis cells (MECs). The specific objectives, mapped onto two
    author-stated research gaps of a recent SPAES/polyimide nanofiber (SPAES/PIN)
    membrane, are: (1) to develop and experimentally validate a molecular-scale
    mechanistic model of proton selectivity using molecular dynamics (MD) that
    predicts the proton transport number and cation crossover from channel geometry
    (Gap 1); (2) to quantitatively characterise the membrane nanostructure to
    parameterise and validate the MD model; and (3) to optimize the proton-conducting
    channels by MD screening of the degree of sulfonation (DS), ion exchange capacity
    (IEC), and side-chain chemistry, confirmed by synthesis and experiment (Gap 2).

2.  Methodology
        The project combines two coupled pillars in a closed predict-verify loop.
    The Japanese team (Tohoku University, Mabuchi Laboratory) performs the MD
    simulations, resolving Grotthuss proton transport with the internationally
    recognised membrane-based empirical valence bond (EVB) method developed by Prof.
    Mabuchi. The Indonesian team (Institut Pertanian Bogor; Universitas Indonesia)
    performs the experiments in Indonesia: membrane synthesis and fabrication,
    nanostructure characterization (SAXS, TEM tomography, PALS, FE-SEM, AFM),
    transport testing, and confirmatory biohydrogen-producing MEC operation. MD
    predicts; experiments verify; the validated model then guides membrane
    optimization.

3.  Expected scientific results of the joint research
        A validated MD-plus-experiment structure-property-transport model that
    reproduces the reference benchmarks (proton transport number t+ = 0.96; 3-10-fold
    lower cation crossover than Nafion-211) from channel geometry, an optimized DS/IEC
    "sweet spot" membrane, design guidelines, and higher biohydrogen production/purity
    in MECs. Outputs: peer-reviewed publications (Q1/Q2) and, where applicable,
    intellectual property.

4.  Mode of cooperation (including exchange of people)
        The teams operate under one common work plan with balanced roles (MD in
    Japan, experiments in Indonesia) and reciprocal data exchange. Young-researcher
    exchange is central: three Universitas Indonesia early-career researchers pursue
    graduate study (master's for the first two years, then doctoral) at Tohoku
    University, and joint workshops/conferences are held annually in each country.

5.  Expected economic and social impact
        Bioenergy from waste: more efficient, lower-cost biohydrogen production
    supports clean-energy transition (Indonesia's Asta Cita bioenergy pillar; Japan's
    bioeconomy 2030), reduces the environmental impact of waste treatment, and builds
    long-term Indonesia-Japan research capacity, aligned with SDG 7, 9, and 13.

PART III.  RESEARCH TOPIC AND WORK PLAN

1.  Detailed Description of the Joint Project

1) Problem statement

        Bioenergy is a key route to a circular, low-carbon economy, turning organic
    waste into usable energy. Microbial electrolysis cells (MECs) are an attractive
    biology-based bioenergy technology: exoelectrogens growing on the anode convert
    organic waste into protons, electrons, and CO2, and with a small applied voltage
    the protons and electrons recombine at the cathode to produce biohydrogen. Typical
    half reactions are:
        Anode:   CH3COOH + 2H2O -> 2CO2 + 8H+ + 8e-;  E deg = -0.28 V (NHE)   (1)
        Cathode: 8H+ + 8e- -> 4H2;                    E deg = -0.42 V (NHE)   (2)

        The proton exchange membrane (PEM) is the heart of a MEC: it separates anode
    and cathode, transports protons, and keeps product gases pure. However, in the
    anolyte various cations (Na+, K+, Mg2+, Ca2+) coexist at concentrations typically
    10^5 times higher than protons and compete for the negatively charged sulfonate
    channels (-HSO3-), hindering proton transport, causing a harmful pH gradient, and
    lowering biohydrogen yield. Therefore, proton selectivity - passing protons while
    excluding competing cations - is the key membrane requirement for MECs.

        A recent sulfonated poly(arylene ether sulfone)/polyimide nanofiber (SPAES/PIN)
    composite membrane achieved outstanding selectivity (proton transport number 0.96;
    3-10-fold lower cation crossover than Nafion-211; 32.4% higher MEC hydrogen
    production). Despite this success, the authors explicitly identified TWO unresolved
    gaps that define the scientific problem of this project:

        Research Gap 1 - No validated mechanistic model for proton selectivity
    [AUTHOR-STATED].
        What the paper did: selectivity was explained only qualitatively; microphase
    separation observed by atomic force microscopy (AFM) in tapping mode was used to
    attribute the effect to "narrower, more branched, more tortuous" hydrophilic
    channels in SPAES relative to Nafion.
        The gap: the authors themselves state that "although no model has yet been
    identified as fully correct, this feature could be explained by the microstructural
    difference." There is no quantitative transport model linking channel geometry to
    the measured proton transport number (0.96) or the 3-10-fold reduction in cation
    crossover. The explanation is descriptive, not predictive.

        Research Gap 2 - Proton-transport channels not yet optimized [AUTHOR-STATED].
        What the paper did: a single SPAES formulation was used (IEC > 1.75 meq/mg,
    degree of sulfonation DS fixed at 100%, base resin polymerized by Kolon).
        The gap: the authors note that "the properties of our developed SPAES/PIN
    composite membrane could be further improved through the optimization of proton
    transport channels in SPAES polymer." No parametric study of DS, IEC, or side-chain
    chemistry was performed; only one point in a large design space was tested.

        Importance and contribution: closing these two gaps converts a proven-but-
    empirical membrane into a rationally designed, predictive technology for biohydrogen
    production. This requires molecular-scale simulation (to see the channels and the
    ion transport) combined with experiments (to synthesise, measure, and validate) -
    exactly the complementary strengths of the Japanese and Indonesian teams.

2) Expected outcomes
        - A validated, molecular-scale mechanistic model (MD + continuum) that predicts
          the proton transport number and cation crossover from channel geometry, and
          reproduces the reference benchmarks (t+ = 0.96; 3-10-fold cation-crossover
          reduction) - closing Gap 1.
        - A quantified DS/IEC and side-chain design map with an identified "sweet spot"
          membrane that maximizes selectivity while retaining tensile strength > 40 MPa -
          closing Gap 2.
        - Demonstrated improvement in biohydrogen production and purity in a MEC using the
          optimized membrane.
        - Scientific outputs: peer-reviewed Q1/Q2 publications and, where applicable,
          registered intellectual property; open design guidelines.
        - Human-capacity outcome: trained early-career researchers (see 5).

3) Scientific and technological challenges and the means and methods to overcome them

        The central challenge is that the existing selectivity explanation is qualitative
    (Gap 1) and the channel chemistry is unoptimized (Gap 2). Prior work (Chae et al.,
    2014; Shin et al., 2017; and the SPAES/PIN source paper) demonstrates the selectivity
    empirically but provides no predictive model and no parametric optimization - these
    have not yet been adequately overcome. This project overcomes them by combining
    molecular dynamics (Japan) with experiments (Indonesia).

    (a) Molecular dynamics methodology (Japan side - Tohoku University, Mabuchi Lab)
        Atomistic models of the SPAES ionomer (baseline DS 100%, IEC > 1.75 meq/mg) are
    built with hydrated sulfonate groups; water content is set from the measured water
    uptake (35 +/- 3%). Classical MD runs in GROMACS 2023 (GPU) with OPLS-AA/GAFF force
    fields and the SPC/E water model; atomic partial charges are derived by the RESP
    method from HF/6-31G* calculations in Gaussian 16. Because proton transport is
    dominated by the Grotthuss (structural) mechanism, proton hopping is treated with the
    membrane-based empirical valence bond (EVB) method developed by Prof. Mabuchi (Tohoku
    University) in its multistate form (MS-EVB), cross-checked by ab initio MD (AIMD/
    DFT-MD) in CP2K 2024. Reactive degradation uses ReaxFF in LAMMPS. Production runs use
    the NPT ensemble (Nose-Hoover thermostat, Parrinello-Rahman barostat) at 298-353 K,

        MD workflow steps (Japan): (1) build the atomistic SPAES model at a target
    DS/IEC; add water and counter-ions (H+, Na+, Mg2+, Ca2+); (2) energy-minimise, then
    equilibrate (NVT then NPT); (3) production MD - radial distribution functions,
    water/ion connectivity, channel width/tortuosity; (4) proton transport (EVB/MS-EVB,
    AIMD) and cation permeation free energy (umbrella sampling) to obtain t+ and
    crossover; (5) fit a continuum Nernst-Planck/Poisson-Boltzmann model to the MD
    outputs for engineering-scale prediction; pass results to Indonesia for validation.

    (b) Experimental methodology (Indonesia side - IPB & Universitas Indonesia)
        The SPAES/PIN composite PEM is fabricated by impregnating polyimide nanofiber
    (PIN) in the centre of a SPAES polymer solution. The SPAES solution uses SPAES of
    > 1.75 meq/mg IEC (base resin polymerized by Kolon, South Korea) and N,N-
    dimethylacetamide (DMAc) as solvent; baseline DS is 100%. For Gap 2, a family of
    SPAES ionomers with varied DS/IEC and side-chain modification (hydroxyl groups;
    n-BuOH self-organization) is synthesised by the Indonesian team. PIN is electrospun
    to ~200 nm. Nafion-211 (DuPont, USA) is the control.

        Experimental protocol steps (Indonesia):
        Step 1. Cast the SPAES/DMAc solution onto the PIN substrate on a glass plate;
                natural drying at 80 degC for 6 h on a clean bench.
        Step 2. Acidify in a 1 N boiling sulfuric acid solution for 2 h; wash with
                deionized water.
        Step 3. Characterise structure: FE-SEM (TESCAN-MIRA3 LMU, Czech) with EDX; AFM
                (tapping mode); SAXS, TEM tomography, and PALS for quantitative channel
                geometry (MD inputs/validation).

4) Future prospect of research
        In the near term the validated model and optimized membrane raise biohydrogen
    yield and purity from waste, lowering cost and environmental impact. In the medium
    term the model enables inverse design (specify a target selectivity; the model
    prescribes the channel chemistry) and extension to multi-cation, real anolytes. In
    the long term it supports an MD-guided membrane library, scale-up, and deployment in
    real MECs and other PEM applications (fuel cells, reverse electrodialysis). The
    synergy is genuine and mutual: Japan contributes world-leading membrane MD (EVB);
    Indonesia contributes bioenergy/experimental capability and real waste feedstocks -
    neither side could deliver the predictive, validated technology alone. Outcomes
    disseminate across ASEAN, supporting Japan's bioeconomy 2030 and Indonesia's Asta
    Cita bioenergy agenda.

5) Exchange plan for young researchers
        Human-capital exchange is a core objective. Three early-career researchers from
    the Energy and Environmental Research Group of Universitas Indonesia will be
    dispatched to Tohoku University for graduate study - a master's degree during the
    first two years of the programme, then continuing to a doctoral degree at Tohoku
    (research capacity building, Japan side):
        - Muhammad Robi Harahap, S.T. (MD of palm-oil-waste PCM nanocomposites).
        - Mahendra Dharma Putra (MD of nanodroplet evaporation/impact for cooling flux).
        - Syadina Aliy (MD of temperature-dependent CO2 capture by ionic liquids).
    In addition, doctoral candidate Erdiyanto Munandar, S.T., M.T. (CO2 direct-air-capture
    contactor design by AI/multiscale modelling) participates from Universitas Indonesia.
    Annual joint workshops are held alternately in Japan and Indonesia, and team members
    present at international conferences. Around 30% of the Japan-side direct expenses are
    allocated to these exchange activities. This mobility builds a lasting Indonesia-Japan
    bioenergy research network and internationally active young researchers.

S.2  RESEARCH ROADMAP

        [Figure S1. Ten-year roadmap of MD-guided proton-selective membranes]
        --------------------------------------------------------------------------
        | Phase 1 (2027-2029): Validated MD model + DS/IEC optimization (closing  |
        |     the two author-stated gaps) with experimental validation.           |
        | Phase 2 (2030-2033): MD extended to multi-cation & operating conditions;|
        |     inverse design of channels for a target selectivity.                |
        | Phase 3 (2034-2037): MD-guided membrane library, scale-up, and          |
        |     deployment in real MECs and other PEM applications.                 |
        --------------------------------------------------------------------------

        This figure illustrates a ten-year roadmap. The first phase (2027-2029) closes
the two author-stated gaps by delivering an MD-validated transport model and an optimized
DS/IEC design space, each confirmed by experiment. The second phase (2030-2033) extends
the model to multi-cation anolytes and real operating conditions and moves toward inverse
design - specifying a target selectivity and letting the MD model prescribe the channel
geometry and DS/IEC. The third phase (2034-2037) uses the model to build a membrane
library, scale up the best formulations, and deploy them in real MECs and other PEM
applications. This roadmap positions MD-guided design as a scalable, sustainable path
aligned with long-term technological goals.

        [Figure S2. Detailed research plan for the funded project]
        --------------------------------------------------------------------------
        | Year 1: Build MD/HPC platform (JP) + facility upgrade (ID, SAXS/TEM/    |

        |         map; synthesis of the "sweet spot" variants (ID); closed-loop   |
        |         experimental validation (ID); blind MD-vs-experiment test.      |
        | Year 3: Inverse design & multi-cation extension (JP); biohydrogen MEC   |
        |         demonstration with the optimized membrane (ID); final model.    |

        In Year 1 the Japanese team builds and benchmarks the MD/HPC platform and the
baseline SPAES/PIN model, while the Indonesian team upgrades characterization, synthesises
the first DS/IEC family, and re-characterises the baseline. In Year 2 the Japanese team
screens side-chain variants and completes the DS/IEC design map, the Indonesian team
synthesises the "sweet spot" variants, and the loop is closed by blind MD-vs-experiment
validation. In Year 3 the model is extended to multi-cation, real-anolyte conditions and
the optimized membrane is demonstrated in a biohydrogen-producing MEC.
