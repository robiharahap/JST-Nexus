================================================================================
RESEARCH GAPS ASSESSMENT
Paper: S.-G. Park, K.-J. Chae, M. Lee, "A sulfonated poly(arylene ether
sulfone)/polyimide nanofiber (SPAES/PIN) composite proton exchange membrane
for microbial electrolysis cell application under the coexistence of diverse
competitive cations and protons," Journal of Membrane Science 540 (2017)
165-173. http://dx.doi.org/10.1016/j.memsci.2017.06.048
================================================================================

PURPOSE
        This document assesses the research gaps that remain in the SPAES/PIN
membrane study and identifies where the work can still be improved. Each gap is
grounded in what the paper actually reported (or did not report), followed by a
concrete direction for improvement. Gaps flagged with [AUTHOR-STATED] are limits
the authors themselves acknowledged in the text; the remainder are inferred from
the methodology, results, and scope of the study.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. NO VALIDATED MECHANISTIC MODEL FOR PROTON SELECTIVITY   [AUTHOR-STATED]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What the paper did:
        Selectivity was explained qualitatively through microphase separation
observed by AFM in tapping mode (Fig. 6), attributing the effect to "narrower,
more branched, more tortuous" hydrophilic channels in SPAES vs. Nafion.
The gap:
        The authors explicitly state "although no model has yet been identified
as fully correct, this feature could be explained by the microstructural
difference." There is no quantitative transport model linking channel geometry
to the measured proton transport number (0.96) or the 3-10-fold reduction in
cation crossover. The explanation is descriptive, not predictive.
How it can be improved:
        - Develop a quantitative ion-transport model (e.g., Nernst-Planck /
          Poisson-Boltzmann, or Grotthuss-vehicular partitioning) that predicts
          t+ and cation crossover from channel dimensions.
        - Add quantitative nanostructure characterization beyond AFM: small-angle
          X-ray scattering (SAXS), TEM tomography, or positron annihilation to
          measure channel width, tortuosity, and cluster connectivity.
        - Correlate measured microstructure with transport data to build a
          structure-property relationship instead of a qualitative narrative.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. PROTON-TRANSPORT CHANNELS NOT YET OPTIMIZED             [AUTHOR-STATED]
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What the paper did:
        A single SPAES formulation was used: IEC >1.75 meq/mg, degree of
sulfonation (DS) fixed at 100%, polymerized by Kolon.
The gap:
        The authors note "the properties of our developed SPAES/PIN composite
membrane could be further improved through the optimization of proton transport
channels in SPAES polymer." No parametric study of DS, IEC, or side-chain
chemistry was performed. Only one point in a large design space was tested.
How it can be improved:
        - Systematically vary DS and IEC and map the trade-off between proton
          conductivity, selectivity, water uptake, and mechanical strength.
        - Explore side-chain modification (e.g., hydroxyl groups as in Kwon et
          al. [34], or self-organization additives as in n-BuOH-treated SPEEK
          [35]) to further narrow/branch the hydrophilic channels.
        - Identify the DS/IEC "sweet spot" that maximizes selectivity without
          sacrificing mechanical stability.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. NANOFIBER SUPPORT MORPHOLOGY NOT PARAMETRICALLY STUDIED
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What the paper did:
        The polyimide nanofiber (PIN) support was electrospun to a single average
fiber thickness of ~200 nm, and the composite reached 21 +/- 2 um total thickness.
The gap:
        Fiber diameter, areal density (loading), porosity, and mat thickness were
not varied. The paper only notes qualitatively that thinner fibers (200 nm here
vs. their earlier SPEEK work [6]) improved impregnation. Since PIN is inactive
for proton transport, its volume fraction directly trades mechanical reinforcement
against conductivity, and this trade-off is unquantified.
How it can be improved:
        - Vary fiber diameter (e.g., 100-500 nm), porosity, and PIN volume
          fraction; measure the resulting impregnation efficiency, tensile
          strength, and proton conductivity to find an optimum.
        - Investigate fiber alignment/orientation and multi-layer architectures.
        - Quantify how thin the composite can go (below 21 um) before mechanical
          reinforcement is lost, since thinner membranes lower internal resistance.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. LONG-TERM DURABILITY AND CHEMICAL/BIOLOGICAL STABILITY UNPROVEN
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What the paper did:
        MECs were operated in fed-batch mode "for over 6 months," with a
three-consecutive-feeding cycle reported for the hydrogen data.
The gap:
        No accelerated aging, no long-term conductivity/selectivity decay curve,
and no chemical degradation analysis (e.g., desulfonation, hydrolysis of the
polyimide, oxidative stability against radical attack) were reported. Hydrocarbon
PEMs like SPAES are known to be more oxidatively vulnerable than perfluorinated
Nafion, yet oxidative (Fenton) stability was not tested.
How it can be improved:
        - Run Fenton's reagent / ex-situ oxidative stability tests and report
          weight loss and property retention.
        - Track proton conductivity, t+, tensile strength, and crossover over
          extended (multi-month to multi-year) continuous operation.
        - Post-mortem analysis (FTIR/NMR/SEM) of aged membranes to identify
          degradation pathways.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. BIOFOULING IN A REAL MICROBIAL ENVIRONMENT NOT ASSESSED
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What the paper did:
        All mass-transfer crossover experiments were "conducted in an uninoculated
cell"; only the final performance test used an inoculated MEC.
The gap:
        In real MECs the anode-facing membrane surface is continuously exposed to
biofilm, extracellular polymeric substances, and cell debris. Membrane biofouling
(which the same group has studied elsewhere for FO membranes, ref [25]) was not
characterized for SPAES/PIN. Crossover and selectivity were measured on clean
membranes, so in-operando degradation of selectivity is unknown.
How it can be improved:
        - Characterize biofilm accumulation and its effect on proton transport
          and crossover over time (confocal microscopy, SEM, impedance).
        - Test anti-fouling surface modifications (e.g., the group's own
          Ag-nanoparticle / polydopamine coating approach [25]).
        - Compare fouling propensity of the hydrocarbon SPAES surface vs. Nafion.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. LIMITED CATION SET AND SIMPLIFIED ANOLYTE CHEMISTRY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What the paper did:
        Cation crossover (Fig. 5) was tested with a standard solution of Na+,
Mg2+, and Ca2+ at 100 mg/L each in an uninoculated cell.
The gap:
        K+ and NH4+ are named in the introduction as relevant competing cations
but were never measured. Real MEC anolytes contain buffers, mixed cations at
varied ratios, and organic species that were not represented. Selectivity was
demonstrated for a narrow, idealized ion set.
How it can be improved:
        - Extend crossover measurements to K+, NH4+, and multivalent transition
          cations, at MEC-realistic concentrations and ratios.
        - Test with real or synthetic wastewater anolytes rather than a
          three-cation standard.
        - Report selectivity coefficients (H+/M+) rather than only relative
          crossover fold-changes.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7. INCONSISTENT / INCOMPLETELY REPORTED TEST CONDITIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What the paper did:
        Proton conductivity (0.17-0.2 S/cm) was measured at 80 degC and 95%
relative humidity (Table 1 footnote c), while the MEC and crossover experiments
ran at 25 +/- 1 degC.
The gap:
        The headline conductivity is reported at fuel-cell conditions (80 degC),
not at the ~25 degC operating condition of the MEC, making the conductivity and
the application data hard to reconcile. Temperature and humidity dependence of
conductivity/selectivity was not mapped.
How it can be improved:
        - Report proton conductivity at the actual MEC operating temperature and
          across a temperature/RH sweep.
        - Provide a single consistent condition set (or a full parametric map) so
          material and application data can be directly compared.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
8. NARROW MEC CONFIGURATION; SCALE-UP AND CONTINUOUS OPERATION UNTESTED
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What the paper did:
        A single two-chambered, fed-batch MEC (180 mL each chamber), carbon-felt
anode (25 cm2), Pt/Ti cathode (0.5 mg Pt/cm2), acetate substrate (3 mM), 0.5 V
applied, was used.
The gap:
        No continuous-flow operation, no stacked/scaled reactors, no larger
membrane areas, and only one substrate (acetate). Membrane behavior under
practical flow, pressure, and current-density ranges is unknown, and membrane
mechanical performance under repeated hydration/dehydration cycling was not tested.
How it can be improved:
        - Evaluate the membrane in continuous-flow and scaled/stacked MECs.
        - Test alternative and complex substrates (real wastewater, fermentation
          effluents) and a range of current densities and applied voltages.
        - Include mechanical cycling (wet/dry, thermal) fatigue testing.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
9. NO TECHNO-ECONOMIC OR MANUFACTURING-SCALE ANALYSIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What the paper did:
        Lower cost than Nafion is claimed qualitatively as a motivation.
The gap:
        No quantified cost comparison, no cost per m2, and no assessment of
whether electrospun-PIN + solution-casting is scalable or reproducible at
manufacturing volumes. The two-step lab process (electrospinning then casting,
acidification, washing) may not translate to roll-to-roll production.
How it can be improved:
        - Perform a techno-economic analysis (materials + processing cost per m2)
          against Nafion and other hydrocarbon PEMs.
        - Demonstrate batch-to-batch reproducibility and a scalable fabrication
          route (e.g., continuous casting on continuous electrospun mat).


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
10. BENCHMARKED ONLY AGAINST NAFION-211
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What the paper did:
        All comparisons were made against a single control, Nafion-211.
The gap:
        The membrane was not benchmarked against other advanced hydrocarbon PEMs,
against the group's own earlier SPEEK/PIN composite [6], or against modified-
Nafion variants cited in the introduction [11]. This makes it hard to judge how
much of the improvement is due to SPAES vs. the nanofiber reinforcement concept
(which is shared with the earlier SPEEK work).
How it can be improved:
        - Add side-by-side benchmarks: SPAES/PIN vs. SPEEK/PIN vs. plain SPAES
          vs. modified Nafion, to isolate the contribution of each element.
        - Include anion-exchange or bipolar membranes as alternative separators
          for MECs.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
11. CLAIMED BROADER APPLICATIONS NOT DEMONSTRATED
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What the paper did:
        The conclusion lists fuel cells, reverse electrodialysis, the chlor-alkali
industry, and gas separation as potential applications.
The gap:
        None of these were experimentally demonstrated; they are asserted as
potential. Requirements differ substantially across these applications (e.g.,
high-temperature fuel cells, high-current RED), so transferability is unproven.
How it can be improved:
        - Provide at least proof-of-concept data in one additional application
          (e.g., a single-cell fuel-cell polarization curve, or an RED stack
          test) to substantiate the broader-applicability claim.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
12. GAS-CROSSOVER TESTING UNDER NON-OPERATING CONDITIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What the paper did:
        Gas crossover (Fig. 7) used a standard gas (5% H2, 70% CH4, 25% CO2) in
water-filled chambers of an uninoculated cell at 25 +/- 1 degC, via a diffusion
gradient.
The gap:
        Crossover was measured by passive diffusion, not under the pressure,
current, and gas-generation dynamics of a running MEC. The reported 2.7-fold
(H2) and twofold (CH4) improvements may not hold under real operating fluxes and
membrane hydration states.
How it can be improved:
        - Measure gas crossover in-operando under representative current density,
          pressure, and hydration.
        - Separate diffusive vs. convective (electro-osmotic drag) contributions
          to crossover.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
13. NO ENVIRONMENTAL / LIFE-CYCLE ASSESSMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
What the paper did:
        Non-fluorinated (fluorine-free) chemistry is presented as an environmental
advantage over Nafion.
The gap:
        No life-cycle assessment, no evaluation of solvent (DMAc is a reproductive
toxicant) handling/recovery, and no end-of-life/recyclability analysis were
provided, despite the sustainability framing.
How it can be improved:
        - Conduct a cradle-to-grave LCA and compare with Nafion.
        - Investigate greener solvents or solvent-recovery for the casting step.
        - Assess membrane recyclability / disposal.

PRIORITY RECOMMENDATION
        The highest-leverage next steps, because they directly extend the paper's
own stated limitations and de-risk practical use, are: (1) a DS/IEC + nanofiber
parametric optimization tied to a quantitative structure-property model
(gaps 1-3), (2) long-term durability and biofouling testing under real MEC
operation (gaps 4-5, 8, 12), and (3) selectivity validation against a realistic
multi-cation anolyte with reported H+/M+ selectivity coefficients (gap 6).
