1   RINGKASAN PROPOSAL

        The global transition toward clean energy has sharply increased the
demand for green hydrogen, and microbial electrolysis cells (MECs) have emerged
as an attractive technology able to convert organic waste into hydrogen gas and
other value-added chemicals with only a small input of electric energy. However,
the proton exchange membrane (PEM) that separates the anode and cathode remains
a major challenge. Conventional Nafion is expensive, allows high crossover of
gases and substrate, and — most critically — loses proton selectivity in the MEC
environment, where competing cations (Na+, K+, Mg2+, Ca2+) coexist with protons
at concentrations typically 10^5 times higher. This project proposes a sulfonated
poly(arylene ether sulfone)/polyimide nanofiber (SPAES/PIN) composite membrane as
an innovative solution. By impregnating SPAES, a proton-conducting hydrocarbon
polymer, into an electrospun polyimide nanofiber support, the membrane combines
superior proton selectivity with strong mechanical and dimensional stability. The
research will study membrane fabrication, physicochemical and electrochemical
properties—such as water uptake, ion exchange capacity, tensile strength, proton
transport number, and cation, substrate, and gas crossover—and validate actual
performance in real hydrogen-producing MECs.
        The project will be led by Korea Maritime and Ocean University, with strong
collaboration from the Green Material Research Group of Kolon Central Research Park.
These partners will contribute expertise in environmental engineering,
bioelectrochemical systems, and advanced polymer membrane synthesis. The
collaboration will also broaden the scope by evaluating the composite membrane in
other applications requiring PEMs, such as fuel cells, reverse electrodialysis, the
chlor-alkali industry, and gas separation. Expected outcomes include scientific
publications, a prototype membrane and MEC test cell, and practical guidelines for
selective PEMs in bioelectrochemical systems. The project also provides training
opportunities for students and young researchers, ensuring knowledge transfer and
capacity building. In the long term, this research supports national goals for clean
hydrogen infrastructure and aligns with the UN Sustainable Development Goals on
energy efficiency, innovation, and climate action.

2   PENDAHULUAN

2.1 Latar belakang masalah

        In recent years, the rapid push toward decarbonisation has been accompanied
by a sharp rise in the demand for reliable, low-cost, and sustainable green-hydrogen
technologies. Microbial electrolysis cells (MECs) represent an attractive green
energy technology that can convert organic waste into a wide range of value-added
products via specific microbes (i.e., exoelectrogens or biocatalysts) with only a
small input of electric energy. Hydrogen gas is the most common product of MECs, but
MECs using biocatalysts can also produce methane, ethanol, butanol, hydrogen
peroxide, and acetate. The basic components of a MEC are two electrodes (anode and
cathode), a membrane, exoelectrogens, and a power supply. In a MEC, exoelectrogens
growing on the anode convert the substrate into protons (H+), electrons (e-), and CO2;
the protons and electrons are then transferred to the cathode and recombine to produce
hydrogen under the assistance of the power supply. Typical half reactions are:
        Anode:   CH3COOH + 2H2O -> 2CO2 + 8H+ + 8e-;  E deg = -0.28 V (NHE)   (1)
        Cathode: 8H+ + 8e- -> 4H2;                    E deg = -0.42 V (NHE)   (2)

        Despite their strategic importance, MECs face distinctive challenges arising
from the proton exchange membrane. A PEM plays the role of a separator between the
anode and cathode to avoid short circuits while transporting protons to the cathode,
and it is essential for improving product purity by preventing the interchange of gases
(CO2, CO, CH4, etc.) generated in both chambers. However, the PEM used in MECs requires
completely different characteristics from that used in hydrogen fuel cells, because

microorganisms are used as biocatalysts in the anode. In the anodic solution of the
MEC, various cations (Na+, Mg2+, Ca2+, etc.) exist at a significantly higher
concentration (typically 10^5 times) than the protons; those cations hinder proton
transport by competing for the negatively charged sulfonate channels (-HSO3-) of the
membrane. As a result of losing the contact chance of protons being transported, protons
accumulate in the anode chamber and cause a serious pH decrease, while a corresponding
pH increase occurs in the cathode chamber. This pH drop adversely affects the anode
bacteria and deteriorates MEC performance. Therefore, proton selectivity—which
selectively passes protons while excluding other competing cations—is a key requirement
for the PEM in MEC applications.

        As a PEM, Nafion (DuPont) is the most widely used; it is a perfluorosulfonic acid
membrane consisting of a hydrophobic fluorocarbon backbone (-CF2-CF2-) and hydrophilic
side chains with sulfonic functional groups (-HSO3). Despite its widespread use, the
Nafion membrane has several limitations for MECs due to its low proton selectivity, the
high crossover tendency of gas and substrate, and its high production cost. To overcome
these drawbacks, sulfonated hydrocarbon-based polymers, mainly sulfonated poly(ether
ether ketone) (SPEEK) and sulfonated poly(arylene ether sulfone) (SPAES), have been
intensively investigated as alternative proton-conducting PEM materials. Compared with
Nafion, SPAES exhibits a significant difference in hydrophobic-hydrophilic microphase
separation, forming more branched, narrower, and more tortuous ion-conducting channels
via the Grotthuss mechanism, which improves proton selectivity while lowering undesirable
gas or substrate crossover.

        Although extensive research has been carried out, several knowledge gaps remain
unresolved. Despite its advantages, SPAES has an inherent mechanical weakness due to an
excessive water uptake (WU) and swelling feature, since the SPAES membrane needs a high
ion exchange capacity (IEC) to obtain proton conductivity comparable to Nafion. This
higher IEC, arising from excessive sulfonation, causes mechanical instability so that the
membrane can easily be delaminated or torn during application. In addition, membrane
morphology strongly affects both electrochemical properties (ion conductivity, water
transport) and membrane stability (water uptake, dimensional swelling, mechanical
strength). These problems show that a comprehensive investigation of a mechanically
reinforced, highly selective PEM is urgently needed.

        The aim of this research is to build a fundamental understanding of proton
selectivity and mechanical reinforcement as a basis for developing efficient and reliable
PEMs for next-generation hydrogen-producing MECs. To solve the physical weakness while
taking advantage of SPAES' unique superior proton transfer characteristics, we develop a
new SPAES/polyimide nanofiber (PIN) composite membrane. In the composite, PINs—highly
compatible with SPAES—are embedded as a substratum in the centre of the SPAES polymer layer
to improve mechanical strength and dimensional stability; the proton is transferred through
the SPAES polymer, while the physical stability is maintained by the PINs. The research
group has previously developed a SPEEK-based composite PEM reinforced with nanofibers,
which forms a strong foundation to further explore the SPAES/PIN membrane. Furthermore, the
proposed research is strengthened through collaboration with the Green Material Research
Group of Kolon Central Research Park, which provides complementary expertise in advanced
polymer synthesis and expands the scope and impact of the study.

        In terms of United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), this research is
aligned with SDG 7 (Affordable and Clean Energy), SDG 9 (Industry, Innovation, and
Infrastructure), and SDG 13 (Climate Action), as it aims to improve energy efficiency,
strengthen clean-hydrogen infrastructure, and reduce the environmental impact of waste
treatment. More importantly, the topic lies at the intersection of two research priority
areas of the EQUITY grant: energy and advanced materials, which also support the national

2.2 Tujuan

        The long term goal of this research is to support the development of efficient and
reliable proton exchange membranes for next-generation hydrogen-producing microbial
electrolysis cells. In general, the objectives of this study are focused as follows:
1.  To fabricate and characterise a SPAES/polyimide nanofiber (PIN) composite proton
    exchange membrane. The study considers the effect of the SPAES ion exchange capacity,
    degree of sulfonation, and nanofiber reinforcement on membrane properties.
2.  To examine how membrane microstructure influences proton selectivity and stability.
    Water uptake, swelling ratio, tensile strength, proton transport number, and microphase
    separation will be tested to optimise selective proton transport.
3.  To evaluate the actual membrane performance in real MECs, including hydrogen production
    efficiency, hydrogen purity, cation crossover, and gas and substrate crossover, in
    comparison with the conventional Nafion-211 membrane.

3   METODOLOGI

        The research will be mainly conducted at the Department of Environmental Engineering,
College of Engineering, Korea Maritime and Ocean University, together with the Green Material
Research Group of Kolon Central Research Park. Over the past decade, the proposing research
group has focused extensively on membrane fabrication and bioelectrochemical systems,
supported by experimental facilities relevant to this topic, as illustrated in Figure 1. In
general, the set-up consists of the membrane fabrication line, the mass-transfer test cell,
the hydrogen-producing MEC reactors, and the material and electrochemical characterisation
devices.

        For MEC application, a SPAES/PIN composite PEM is fabricated by impregnating PIN in
the centre of a SPAES polymer solution. The SPAES polymer solution is prepared using SPAES of
>1.75 meq/mg IEC, polymerized by Kolon (South Korea), and N,N-dimethylacetamide (DMAc) as a
solvent; the degree of sulfonation (DS)—incorporation of -HSO3 groups into the polymer main
chain—is 100%. As a supporting substrate, PIN is selected due to its excellent mechanical
properties and compatibility with SPAES, and it is fabricated by the electrospinning method
with an average fiber thickness of 200 nm. The composite membrane is prepared by pouring the
SPAES/DMAc polymer solution onto the PIN substrate on a glass plate, followed by natural drying
at 80 degC for 6 h on a clean bench. The membranes are then acidified in a 1 N boiling sulfuric
acid solution for 2 h and washed with deionized water.

crosshead speed of 50 mm/min (ASTM D882).

(a) Pelaksanaan penelitian di PT-Pengusul

        The proposed research aims to develop and validate a highly selective SPAES/PIN
composite membrane for hydrogen-producing MECs. The main activities are listed as follows.

    A.  Upgrading of the Current Facility for Composite Membrane Fabrication
            The initial step of this study is to upgrade the existing membrane fabrication
        facility. Over the past decade, continuous improvements have been made to enhance
        membrane quality and deepen the analysis. In this research, several steps will be
        conducted. First, the electrospinning unit and the solution-casting line will be
        upgraded. This allows more uniform polyimide nanofiber mats (~200 nm) and more
        reproducible SPAES/DMAc impregnation to be produced. In addition, one of our future
        objectives is to investigate multi-layer composites; hence, another crucial aspect is
        to develop a casting head that can produce ultrathin membranes (~21 um) uniformly.

    B.  Experimental Study of Membrane Properties on Various Fabrication Conditions
            The experimental study of membrane properties is one of the main work packages in
        this project. This part of the research is directed at exploring how the membrane
        behaves under a variety of fabrication and operational conditions. By conducting
        systematic experiments, the study will highlight the key features of proton selectivity
        that are often difficult to capture in theoretical or numerical approaches. Careful
        observation will ensure that each stage of the membrane behaviour is well documented.
            The main objective is to produce high quality data that represents membrane
        phenomena across a wide range of conditions. Parameters such as SPAES ion exchange
        capacity, degree of sulfonation, and nanofiber thickness will be adjusted one by one to
        see their influence. In addition, combinations of these parameters will be explored to
        reveal more complex behaviour. Through this approach, the research will map the
        conditions that strongly affect water uptake, swelling, and proton transport.
            The experimental data will then be analysed to identify patterns and key trends in
        membrane behaviour. This includes comparing how different conditions influence water
        uptake, swelling ratio, and tensile strength. The analysis will also look for critical
        thresholds, for example the ion exchange capacity beyond which mechanical instability
        occurs. By combining these observations, the study can provide a clearer explanation of
        the mechanisms that govern selective proton transport.

            The outcomes of this study are expected to provide useful insights into the
        fundamental mechanisms of selective proton transport in composite membranes. The
        collected data will serve as a reliable reference not only for this project but also
        for future studies in related fields. In the longer term, these findings can contribute
        to improving membrane transport models and guiding the design of applications where
        selective ion transport plays an important role.

    C.  Examining the Microstructure Modification Method to Improve Proton Selectivity
            Microstructure modification is an important method to enhance proton selectivity.
        By altering the microphase separation or the nanofiber reinforcement, the interaction
        between the ion-conducting clusters and the competing cations can be controlled. This
        directly influences how protons hop through the sulfonic acid channels, which in turn
        affects the overall selectivity and MEC performance.
            The study will compare different types of microstructure modifications, such as
        changes in degree of sulfonation, ion exchange capacity, or nanofiber embedding. Each
        modified membrane will be tested under controlled conditions to observe its effect on
        the proton transport number and the crossover of cations, substrate, and gas. These
        comparisons will make it possible to identify the microstructures that provide the most
        significant improvement in proton selectivity.
            The results will then be analysed to establish clear links between microstructure
        and selectivity enhancement. Attention will be given to determining which features are
        most effective—whether narrower, more branched, or more tortuous hydrophilic channels.
        The findings are expected to give valuable insights that can support the design of more
        efficient membranes in the future.

    D.  Early development of the Hydrogen-Producing MEC Test Cell
            The development of a reliable MEC test cell is an essential step in this project.
        The cell is expected to operate with controlled temperature, applied voltage, and
        substrate feeding. Having a stable and repeatable test platform is important to ensure
        that the experiments can be carried out under consistent conditions. This step also
        helps to reduce uncertainties in the later analysis of membrane performance. Previous
        studies have provided several methods that can be used as a starting point of this work
        package.
            In the early stage, the focus will be on designing a simple but effective system.
        A two-chambered MEC (180 mL working volume each) is operated in fed-batch mode at
        25 +/- 1 degC. The anode for exoelectrogen enrichment is carbon felt (25 cm^2), and the
        cathode is a Pt-loaded perforated titanium plate (0.5 mg Pt/cm^2) of the same size. A
        nutrient mineral buffer (pH 7.0) is used as the anolyte and phosphate-buffered solution
        (pH 7.0) as the catholyte, with acetate (3 mM) as the substrate; a voltage of 0.5 V is
        applied using a DC power supply. Other options, such as continuous-flow operation, may
        also be explored if needed.
            The outcome of this development will provide a strong foundation for further
        improvement. Once a stable single-cell system is achieved, the design can be extended to
        stacked or continuous configurations. In the long run, the MEC test cell will become a
        key tool to support systematic studies of membrane selectivity and hydrogen production.

(b) Pelaksanaan penelitian di PT-Mitra (maksimum 1 halaman tiap peneliti mitra)

    1)  Korea Maritime and Ocean University (Bioelectrochemical Systems Group)
            To enhance the research knowledge and impact of the proposed study, several
        collaborations will be established. The first collaboration is with Dr. Sung-Gwan Park
        of the Department of Environmental Engineering, Korea Maritime and Ocean University. His
        expertise covers microbial electrolysis cells, bioelectrochemical systems, and mass
        transport across proton exchange membranes, with a strong track record in developing
        efficient and sustainable hydrogen-producing systems. He has also been actively involved
        in research on selective inhibition of methanogens, cation crossover analysis, and
        thermoeconomic evaluation of MECs. With this background, Dr. Park will contribute
        valuable knowledge and experience to support the development of advanced and highly
        selective membrane strategies.
            This collaboration will focus on evaluating the possibility of applying the SPAES/PIN
        membrane to other bioelectrochemical systems, such as microbial fuel cells, osmotic
        microbial fuel cells, or reverse electrodialysis cells. Based on his expertise in
        bioelectrochemical systems, Dr. Park will lead the technical evaluation to explore how the
        composite membrane can be integrated into these applications. The study will not only
        consider the feasibility of implementation but also assess potential benefits in terms of
        proton selectivity, reliability, and operational cost reduction. Such an evaluation is
        important to understand the wider opportunities of the membrane beyond its use in MECs.

        is expected to provide valuable data that can guide the design of future prototypes.
        Ultimately, the collaboration aims to broaden the application of the SPAES/PIN membrane and

8   PETA JALAN (ROAD MAP) RISET

        [Figure 4. Ten years roadmap of selective PEMs for MEC hydrogen production]
        --------------------------------------------------------------------------
        | Phase 1 (2025-2027): Fundamentals & initial development                 |
        | Phase 2 (2028-2031): Further exploration & conceptual design            |
        | Phase 3 (2032-2036): Scientific advancement & technology maturation     |
        --------------------------------------------------------------------------

        This figure illustrates a ten-year roadmap outlining the phased development of
selective proton exchange membranes for MEC hydrogen production. The first phase
(2025-2027) focuses on fundamentals and initial development, including upgrading the
current fabrication facility, testing SPAES/PIN membranes under various fabrication
conditions, microstructure modification, and early MEC test-cell development. The second
phase (2028-2031) moves into further exploration and conceptual design, emphasising detailed
studies on proton selectivity and microphase separation, guidelines for membrane
reinforcement and MEC operation, and working system prototypes. Finally, the third phase
(2032-2036) highlights scientific advancement and technology maturation, aiming to establish
transport models, mass-production strategies for advanced composite membranes, and devices
tested in real environments that meet reliability standards. This roadmap positions the
SPAES/PIN membrane as a scalable and sustainable solution aligned with long-term
technological goals.

        This figure presents a two-year research roadmap tailored to the proposed study,
breaking down specific tasks into measurable milestones. In Year 1, the focus is on upgrading
the current facility by integrating an improved electrospinning and solution-casting line,
FE-SEM/EDX/AFM characterisation, re-adjustment of the acidification and washing steps, and
redesigning the casting head. Concurrently, experiments on single-layer membrane phenomena
(ion exchange capacity, degree of sulfonation, nanofiber thickness) and microstructure mapping
are conducted, supported by trials of a uniform casting method. In Year 2, the scope expands to
proton transport number and crossover analysis, MEC operation, and comparison of the SPAES/PIN
membrane with Nafion-211. Further, advanced microstructures such as higher-DS and thinner-
nanofiber designs are explored, alongside first-trial MEC experiments under diverse conditions.
This structured plan ensures systematic progression from fundamental membrane analysis to more
complex MEC scenarios, forming a strong foundation for future large-scale implementation.
